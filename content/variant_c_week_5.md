# Variant C — Week 5: Pharma Education

**Profile:** Lifelong / Hypotonic PE
**Week 5 theme:** *What you need to know to have an informed conversation with a doctor.*
**Total sessions:** 7 (5 new educational, 2 lighter integration days)
**Average duration:** 6–9 minutes
**Voice direction:** Same Adam / Bella. Slightly more clinical-direct this week — we're conveying medical information and the user needs accuracy more than warmth. Not cold, but precise.

**Content philosophy:** This is pure educational content about the two main pharma options for PE — dapoxetine and topical lidocaine. The week does not recommend either option. It does not steer the user toward a decision. It provides the information needed for an informed conversation with a urologist or sexual medicine specialist, who is the only person actually qualified to make a clinical recommendation.

**Critical compliance notes for production:**

1. The app must include an explicit disclaimer that the educational content does not constitute medical advice. Recommend a brief on-screen disclaimer banner pinned during week 5 sessions.
2. Dapoxetine is approved in the EU, UK, and many other markets, but is NOT FDA-approved in the United States. US users should be told this directly.
3. Behavioral practice (stop-start, pelvic floor work) continues alongside this educational content.
4. The pharma sessions should be skippable for users who indicated at intake they're not interested in pharma options.

# Session 30 — "How PE pharma works"

**Day:** 30
**Duration:** ~7 min
**Type:** Educational
**Goal:** Explain the two main mechanical approaches — central serotonergic modulation (SSRIs) and peripheral sensitivity reduction (topical anesthetics). Set up the specific drugs covered later in the week.

---

Today, the underlying mechanics.

[PAUSE 2s]

There are two fundamentally different ways pharma can extend ejaculatory latency. They work in completely different places in the body, with completely different mechanisms.

[PAUSE 3s]

[firmer]
Approach one — central serotonergic modulation.

[PAUSE 2s]

The brain pathway that gates ejaculation runs largely on serotonin. More specifically, when serotonin levels in certain pathways are higher, the ejaculation reflex fires later. Less serotonin available, faster reflex.

[PAUSE 2s]

Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors — SSRIs — are a class of drug that increases serotonin availability in synaptic spaces by blocking its reuptake. They were originally developed and used as antidepressants, but they were observed early on to have a side effect of delayed ejaculation. That side effect, originally a problem in patients taking them for depression, turned out to be useful for men with PE.

[PAUSE 2s]

Most SSRIs are designed for daily, long-term use — they work by maintaining steady-state levels in the body. The problem for PE: daily SSRI use produces other side effects beyond ejaculation delay, like reduced libido, possible erectile dysfunction, and the risk of withdrawal symptoms when stopping. Not great for men whose only issue is timing.

[PAUSE 2s]

Dapoxetine was specifically engineered to be taken on demand — a few hours before sex, not daily — so that the ejaculation-delaying effect happens when you want it without the steady-state side effects. We'll cover dapoxetine specifically tomorrow.

[PAUSE 3s]

[firmer]
Approach two — peripheral sensitivity reduction.

[PAUSE 2s]

The other approach doesn't touch the brain at all. It works at the level of the penis itself. The glans — the head of the penis — has very high nerve density. The thinking is: if you slightly reduce that sensitivity, the ejaculation reflex receives less input, and the threshold for triggering ejaculation gets further away.

[PAUSE 2s]

Topical anesthetics — lidocaine, sometimes combined with prilocaine — applied to the glans before sex temporarily reduce nerve sensitivity in the application area. The effect is local and brief, lasting about twenty to forty-five minutes after application.

[PAUSE 2s]

This approach has been studied since the 1960s and has decades of clinical history. We'll cover it specifically the day after tomorrow.

[PAUSE 3s]

A few honest observations about both approaches.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Both work. Clinical trials over decades show statistically significant IELT improvements over placebo for both classes. Dapoxetine roughly doubles or triples baseline IELT in many users. Topical lidocaine similar magnitude, sometimes greater.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Both have side effects. Dapoxetine — nausea, dizziness, headache, fatigue are the most common. Topical lidocaine — possible decreased sensation that some men find unwelcome, possible transfer of the anesthetic to a partner if a condom isn't used.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Neither is a cure. Pharma extends IELT during the period the medication is active. It doesn't change underlying wiring. If you stop the medication, baseline returns. This is part of why behavioral work matters — behavioral skills are durable, pharma effects are temporal.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Both are commonly combined with behavioral work. Many men with lifelong PE use one or the other as a bridge while behavioral skills develop, with the goal of eventually relying less on pharma over time.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow, dapoxetine specifically.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work today.

# Session 31 — "Dapoxetine"

**Day:** 31
**Duration:** ~9 min
**Type:** Educational
**Goal:** Specific information on dapoxetine — what it is, how it's taken, what to expect in terms of efficacy, side effects, contraindications, and approval status.

---

Today, dapoxetine.

[PAUSE 3s]

The basics.

[PAUSE 2s]

Dapoxetine is a short-acting selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor. It was specifically developed for the treatment of premature ejaculation, unlike most other SSRIs which were developed for depression and used off-label for PE.

[PAUSE 2s]

Brand names vary by country. The most common is Priligy. Generic versions exist in many markets.

[PAUSE 3s]

How it's taken.

[PAUSE 2s]

On demand. One to three hours before anticipated sex. Not daily.

[PAUSE 2s]

Two doses are standard — thirty milligrams and sixty milligrams. Most users start at thirty and increase to sixty if needed and tolerated. Maximum frequency is once every twenty-four hours.

[PAUSE 3s]

What to expect in terms of efficacy.

[PAUSE 2s]

The largest published clinical trial, involving over a thousand men across twenty-two countries, found mean IELT increased from about a minute at baseline to approximately three minutes on thirty milligrams, and three and a half minutes on sixty milligrams, after twelve weeks of as-needed use. That's roughly a two-to-three-fold increase compared to baseline.

[PAUSE 2s]

Note that the placebo group in that trial also saw improvement — from about one minute to two minutes. So the drug's added benefit beyond placebo is meaningful but not dramatic. This is true of most PE pharma.

[PAUSE 2s]

Individual response varies significantly. Some users see much larger improvements; some see less than the average. Whether dapoxetine works well for any specific person is genuinely individual and only knowable by trying it.

[PAUSE 3s]

Side effects.

[PAUSE 2s]

The most common, in order of frequency: nausea, dizziness, headache, diarrhea, somnolence — feeling sleepy or fatigued.

[PAUSE 2s]

Most are mild and transient — they happen during the few hours the drug is active, then resolve. Some users find them tolerable; others don't. Approximately four percent of users on thirty milligrams discontinued the drug due to side effects in clinical trials. About eight percent on sixty milligrams.

[PAUSE 2s]

A specific risk worth knowing about: orthostatic hypotension. This means dizziness or fainting when standing up quickly, due to a temporary blood pressure drop. The risk is highest in users who are dehydrated, who haven't eaten recently, or who consume alcohol with the drug. Not usually serious, but worth knowing about if you decide to try it.

[PAUSE 3s]

Contraindications.

[PAUSE 2s]

Dapoxetine should not be combined with other SSRIs, MAOIs, or several other classes of psychiatric medication — the risk is serotonin syndrome, which is rare but serious.

[PAUSE 2s]

It should not be used by men with significant heart conditions, severe kidney or liver impairment, or a history of mania or seizures.

[PAUSE 2s]

These contraindications are why the doctor's evaluation matters. The doctor will know your medical history and can identify whether dapoxetine is safe for you specifically. Self-prescribing through online sources skips this safety step.

[PAUSE 3s]

Approval status.

[PAUSE 2s]

Approved in the European Union since 2009. Available in the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, most of Asia, and many other markets. Often available without prescription in some countries, by prescription in others.

[PAUSE 2s]

Not currently FDA-approved in the United States. US users may obtain it through specialty providers — telehealth services that source it internationally, sometimes — but the regulatory pathway matters. Discuss this with a US-based physician before pursuing those routes.

[PAUSE 3s]

A common question.

[PAUSE 2s]

Some men ask whether they could just take a daily SSRI like paroxetine or sertraline instead of dapoxetine, since those are widely available. The honest answer is yes, it's a thing some doctors prescribe off-label, and it works — but the side effect profile of daily SSRI use is more significant than on-demand dapoxetine, and the stop-and-start dynamics of daily SSRIs are different. If you're going to discuss SSRIs with a doctor, ask specifically about both options, with their real trade-offs.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow, topical lidocaine.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work today.

# Session 32 — "Topical lidocaine"

**Day:** 32
**Duration:** ~8 min
**Type:** Educational
**Goal:** Specific information on topical lidocaine and lidocaine-prilocaine formulations.

---

Today, topical lidocaine.

[PAUSE 3s]

The basics.

[PAUSE 2s]

Topical anesthetics applied to the penis before sex temporarily reduce nerve sensitivity in the area where they're applied. The two most common formulations for PE are lidocaine alone and lidocaine combined with prilocaine — a related anesthetic that extends the duration of the numbing effect.

[PAUSE 2s]

These are applied as creams, gels, or sprays. The sprays are a more recent development specifically formulated for PE, with brand names including Fortacin and Promescent in various markets.

[PAUSE 3s]

How they're used.

[PAUSE 2s]

Apply to the glans — the head of the penis, particularly the underside and the frenulum — about fifteen to thirty minutes before sex. Cream formulations typically need longer; sprays often work in five to ten minutes.

[PAUSE 2s]

A condom is recommended. The reason is transfer. If the anesthetic is still active on the surface when penetration happens, some of it can transfer to the partner, partially numbing them. Most partners find this unwelcome. A condom prevents the transfer.

[PAUSE 2s]

Some newer spray formulations claim less transfer risk, but the safest practice is still condom use unless your specific formulation has been tested for that and your doctor has confirmed it's appropriate to skip.

[PAUSE 2s]

Excess can be wiped off before sex, which reduces transfer risk further.

[PAUSE 3s]

What to expect in terms of efficacy.

[PAUSE 2s]

Multiple clinical trials and meta-analyses show statistically significant IELT improvements with topical anesthetics versus placebo. Magnitude varies — some studies show two-to-three-fold improvement, similar to dapoxetine. Some show greater.

[PAUSE 2s]

The 2003 study of lidocaine-prilocaine spray, for example, found IELT increased from about one minute baseline to about six to seven minutes with treatment.

[PAUSE 2s]

A practical note: topical anesthetics are often more effective for men whose primary issue is glans hypersensitivity — meaning sensation is intense enough that the reflex fires before there's time to manage it. For men whose issue is more about central reflex timing, dapoxetine may work better. Most men don't know which they are without trying both.

[PAUSE 3s]

Side effects.

[PAUSE 2s]

Most users report no systemic side effects — these are local applications, very little gets absorbed into general circulation.

[PAUSE 2s]

Local side effects can include: a slight tingling or burning sensation when first applied (usually transient); decreased sensation that some men find unwelcome — feeling less of the experience than they want to feel; partner transfer if condom or wiping isn't used.

[PAUSE 2s]

A specific concern for some users: the decreased sensation can sometimes go too far, making it hard to ejaculate at all during the active period of the medication. This isn't dangerous, but if it happens repeatedly, the dose or formulation may need adjusting.

[PAUSE 2s]

Allergic reactions to lidocaine or prilocaine specifically are rare but possible. If you've had reactions to dental anesthetics, mention this to your doctor — many of those use the same drug class.

[PAUSE 3s]

Contraindications.

[PAUSE 2s]

Generally fewer than dapoxetine, since topical doesn't enter general circulation in significant amounts.

[PAUSE 2s]

Anyone with a history of allergic reaction to local anesthetics should avoid these. Anyone with significant skin conditions in the genital area should discuss application with a doctor first.

[PAUSE 2s]

Some methemoglobinemia risk exists with prilocaine specifically in people with certain underlying conditions, but this is rare with normal use.

[PAUSE 3s]

Approval status.

[PAUSE 2s]

Lidocaine and lidocaine-prilocaine formulations are widely approved across most markets, including the US. Some PE-specific spray formulations are approved in some markets and not others. Local availability varies.

[PAUSE 2s]

Many topical lidocaine products are available over the counter or through telehealth services without prescription in most markets, but a doctor's involvement is still recommended for assessing whether topical use is appropriate for your specific situation, and for selecting the right formulation.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow, the conversation with the doctor.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work today.

# Session 33 — "The doctor conversation"

**Day:** 33
**Duration:** ~8 min
**Type:** Educational + practical guidance
**Goal:** Walk through how to have the actual conversation with a doctor about pharma options.

---

Today, how to have the conversation with a doctor.

[PAUSE 2s]

If you've decided you're interested in exploring pharma — even just exploring, not committing — this session helps you set up the conversation.

[PAUSE 3s]

Who to talk to.

[PAUSE 2s]

The right specialty for this is urology or sexual medicine. Some primary care physicians are comfortable handling PE; others aren't. If your primary doctor seems uncertain, ask for a referral to urology.

[PAUSE 2s]

Telehealth services that focus on men's sexual health are also legitimate options, with the caveat that they vary widely in quality. Look for services that include an actual physician evaluation, not just a questionnaire-and-prescription model. Online services that ship medication after a brief form-fill should generally be approached with caution.

[PAUSE 3s]

What to bring to the conversation.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Your medical history. Especially current medications, including any psychiatric medication, and any history of cardiovascular issues, kidney or liver problems, seizures, or mania. These affect what's safe for you specifically.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
A clear description of your PE. How long you've had it. Whether it's lifelong or acquired. What your typical IELT is. What you've tried so far, including this protocol.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Specific questions you want answered. Don't rely on the doctor to walk you through everything if there's something specific you want to know.

[PAUSE 3s]

Questions worth asking, depending on your direction.

[PAUSE 2s]

If you're interested in dapoxetine: Is it available in this market? What dose would you start with? What side effects should I watch for? What should I do if the side effects are intolerable? Are there interactions with anything I'm currently taking?

[PAUSE 2s]

If you're interested in topical anesthetics: What specific formulation do you recommend? With or without a condom? How long before sex should I apply? What if the numbing is too strong?

[PAUSE 2s]

If you're undecided: Given my situation, which would you start with and why? Are there reasons one might not work for me specifically?

[PAUSE 2s]

A useful general question regardless: What would the next step be if this option doesn't work or has unacceptable side effects?

[PAUSE 3s]

What to expect from the doctor.

[PAUSE 2s]

A reasonable doctor will: ask questions about your history before prescribing anything; discuss the trade-offs with you; explain what to expect in terms of efficacy and side effects; check for contraindications; and follow up after some weeks to see how it's going.

[PAUSE 2s]

A doctor who: prescribes immediately without questions; doesn't discuss alternatives; doesn't follow up; or seems to push a specific brand strongly — those are signs to consider a second opinion.

[PAUSE 3s]

A subtle point that matters.

[PAUSE 2s]

The conversation about PE can feel embarrassing. Most men anticipate this and dread it. In practice, urologists and sexual medicine specialists hear about PE from patients constantly — it's one of the most common things they treat. The conversation is much less awkward in the room than it is in your imagination beforehand.

[PAUSE 2s]

If your doctor seems uncomfortable with the topic — that's the doctor's problem, not yours, and a sign to find a different one. Sexual health professionals should be able to discuss this matter-of-factly.

[PAUSE 3s]

A note on combining pharma with this protocol.

[PAUSE 2s]

If you do start a pharma option, the behavioral work doesn't stop. Continue stop-start sessions, continue pelvic floor work, continue the breath practice. The combination — pharma plus behavioral — has stronger evidence than either alone.

[PAUSE 2s]

In fact, many men start pharma during week five or week six of a behavioral protocol and gradually use it less as behavioral skills mature. The pharma is a bridge, not a destination.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow, back to behavioral practice.

[PAUSE 2s]
