# Variant A — Week 3: Bringing It In

**Week 3 theme:** *From solo skill to partnered skill (or deeper solo work).*
**Total sessions:** 7
**Average duration:** 6–9 minutes
**Voice direction:** Same Adam / Bella. Slightly firmer this week — the user is moving from internal practice to interpersonal practice (or deeper internal work). Confidence in the voice helps the user trust the structure.

**Critical structural note:** Week 3 branches based on whether the user has a partner participating. The app should detect this from intake and serve the right session sequence. Three paths now exist (partnered-yes, partnered-declined, solo), all written below.

---

## Branch logic

- **All users**: Sessions 15, 16, 17, 21 (these work for all paths)
- **Partnered users with willing partner**: Sessions 18p, 19p, 20p
- **Partnered users whose partner declined**: Sessions 18n, 19n, 20n (added based on QA findings — the original v1 design left these users to fall back to solo content, which under-served them)
- **Single users / no partner**: Sessions 18s, 19s, 20s

The app surfaces the right path based on a Day 14 question: "Do you have a partner who's open to doing some practices with you starting this week?" — three options: yes / asked and they declined / no partner currently. The app should also offer a "switch paths" affordance so that a user who started on path-p can switch to path-n if their partner says no after Day 18p, and a user on path-n can switch to path-p if their partner later changes their mind.

[PAUSE 2s]

# Session 18s — "Solo work, longer" (SOLO PATH)

**Day:** 18, solo path
**Duration:** ~6 min (audio) for 25–30 min self-directed practice
**Type:** Practice (extended solo session)
**Goal:** Longer-format solo work for users without a participating partner. Combines breath, sensate focus elements, arousal scale work in one session.

---

Today, a longer self-directed solo session.

[PAUSE 2s]

You'll set aside thirty minutes. Privacy. No interruptions.

[PAUSE 3s]

Structure:

[PAUSE 2s]

First five minutes — body scan with extended exhale breathing. The same six-zone scan from yesterday. Drop into parasympathetic state.

[PAUSE 2s]

Next ten minutes — solo sensate focus. Touch your body slowly, with attention. This is different from the sensate focus session in week two, where genitals were excluded — that one was about reopening the wider field of sensation. Here, we're integrating, so genitals are in but not the focus. Vary touch quality — light, firm, slow, brief. No goal of arousal or non-arousal. Just sensation.

[PAUSE 2s]

Next ten minutes — solo stop-start. Climb to a six. Hold. Stop completely. Three slow exhales. Wait until you're back at four. Resume. Repeat three to four times.

[PAUSE 2s]

Last five minutes — back to breath. Three minutes of extended exhale to come down. Two minutes of just lying there, paying attention to the body's state.

[PAUSE 3s]

What this longer session does that shorter sessions can't.

[PAUSE 2s]

It builds endurance for arousal awareness. Most men, even with practice, lose attentional clarity after about ten minutes of arousal — the system gets tired and reverts to old patterns. By extending the session, you train through that fatigue. The skill consolidates more durably.

[PAUSE 2s]

It also gives the body more time in the five-to-seven arousal range. The sweet spot we want to expand.

[PAUSE 3s]

A few practical notes.

[PAUSE 2s]

Finishing the session is optional. If you want to ejaculate at the end, fine. If you don't, also fine. Both have their place.

[PAUSE 2s]

If you find yourself losing attention at minute fifteen, don't push through with grit. Take a breath, restart attention, accept that the second half won't be as sharp as the first. The reps still count.

[PAUSE 2s]

This is a once-a-week practice. Don't do it more than that. The body needs days between extended sessions to consolidate.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow continues solo work, focused.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work today.

# Session 19p — "Sensate focus, partnered" (PARTNERED PATH)

**Day:** 19, partnered path
**Duration:** ~10 min
**Type:** Lesson with full practice walkthrough
**Goal:** Walk both partners through their first sensate focus session together.

---

Today, the first partnered practice. Sensate focus together.

[PAUSE 2s]

This session is meant to be listened to with your partner, ideally just before doing the practice. Or, if you prefer, you can listen alone and then explain it to them. Either works.

[PAUSE 3s]

Here's the practice.

[PAUSE 2s]

Set aside thirty minutes. A bedroom or somewhere private and comfortable. Soft light. No phones in reach. No agenda about what comes after.

[PAUSE 2s]

Both of you undress to whatever feels comfortable. Underwear is fine. Naked is fine. Not a sexual undressing — just a comfortable one.

[PAUSE 2s]

The practice is in two halves of about fifteen minutes each.

[PAUSE 3s]

[firmer]
Part one. One person is the giver, one is the receiver.

[PAUSE 2s]

The giver touches the receiver — slowly, with attention. The whole body, except for genitals and breasts. Arms. Hands. Legs. Back. Chest. Face. Hair.

[PAUSE 2s]

The giver's job is to touch in whatever way feels interesting. Light. Firm. Slow drag. The giver is exploring, not performing.

[PAUSE 2s]

The receiver's job is to receive. Pay attention to what they feel. They don't need to give feedback verbally during the session — though they can if a touch is uncomfortable, in which case they say so directly.

[PAUSE 3s]

Three rules for part one.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
One. No genitals or breasts. Today's session is about reopening the wider field of sensation. We'll add those areas in later sessions.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Two. No goal of arousal. If arousal happens for either person, fine. If not, also fine. Don't try to arouse or be aroused.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Three. No reciprocation pressure. The receiver does not have to do anything for the giver. They're receiving. That's the whole role.

[PAUSE 3s]

After fifteen minutes, switch. Receiver becomes giver. Giver becomes receiver.

[PAUSE 3s]

After both halves, take five minutes together. No structure. Talk if you want. Don't talk if you don't want.

[PAUSE 3s]

What to expect.

[PAUSE 2s]

The first sensate focus session often feels awkward. Slow touch with no goal feels strange. Your partner may laugh nervously. You may laugh nervously. That's fine — laugh and continue.

[PAUSE 2s]

You may both find it surprisingly intimate. More intimate than sex. That's a common reaction. Sensate focus removes the performance frame and reveals what's underneath.

[PAUSE 2s]

You may also find it boring. That's also fine. Boredom in sensate focus is a feature, not a bug — it means the goal-bound tension is releasing.

[PAUSE 3s]

A few things to avoid.

[PAUSE 2s]

Don't escalate to sex. The session ends when the second half ends. If sex follows organically later, that's between the two of you — but the practice itself doesn't include sex. Keep these separate or the goal-bound frame creeps back.

[PAUSE 2s]

Don't comment a lot during the giver/receiver halves. Some communication is fine. But if you're constantly checking in — "is this okay? does that feel good?" — you're back in the performance frame.

[PAUSE 3s]

This session, repeated weekly, is one of the most powerful interventions there is for performance-anxiety-driven PE. Once a week is the prescription. More than once a week dilutes it.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow, partnered stop-start.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work today.

# Session 19s — "Solo edging foundation" (SOLO PATH)

**Day:** 19, solo path
**Duration:** ~7 min (audio) for 20–25 min self-directed practice
**Type:** Practice (solo edging)
**Goal:** Introduce solo edging as a practice mode. Multiple stop-start cycles in one session, training endurance.

---

Today, edging.

[PAUSE 2s]

Edging is solo stop-start, extended. Multiple cycles. The point is to spend more time in the high-arousal range — six, seven — without crossing into eight.

[PAUSE 3s]

Set aside twenty to twenty-five minutes. Privacy. Time. The same setup as previous solo sessions.

[PAUSE 3s]

Structure.

[PAUSE 2s]

Three minutes of breath to start.

[PAUSE 2s]

Then begin solo arousal work. Light grip. Varied pace.

[PAUSE 2s]

Climb to six. Stay there. Don't immediately stop — try to *hold* at six for thirty to sixty seconds before backing down. This is the new skill — staying at high arousal rather than instantly retreating.

[PAUSE 3s]

When you can no longer hold at six without crossing toward seven, stop. Three slow exhales. Drop to four.

[PAUSE 2s]

Then climb again. This time, climb to seven. Hold there for fifteen to thirty seconds. Stop. Drop. Resume.

[PAUSE 3s]

Then back to six. Hold. Drop. Resume.

[PAUSE 2s]

Continue this pattern for fifteen to twenty minutes. Mix sixes and sevens. Always stop before eight.

[PAUSE 3s]

Finish the session or stop without finishing — both fine.

[PAUSE 3s]

A few specifics.

[PAUSE 2s]

Holding at six is harder than dropping from six. The instinct is to immediately stop the moment you notice you're at six. The new skill is to stay there — let your body practice arousal-without-progression.

[PAUSE 2s]

Holding at seven is significantly harder. The reflex starts pulling you toward eight. Use breath actively — long exhales while you're at seven actually keep you there longer, by tilting the nervous system slightly back toward parasympathetic.

[PAUSE 3s]

Don't try seven if six isn't reliably holdable yet. Walk before you run. If your first session is all six-work, that's a complete session.

[PAUSE 3s]

Frequency: once or twice a week. Not daily. Edging more often than that fatigues the system without consolidation.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow we work on a specific cognitive skill that pairs with edging.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work today.

# Session 20p — "Partnered stop-start" (PARTNERED PATH)

**Day:** 20, partnered path
**Duration:** ~9 min
**Type:** Lesson with practice walkthrough
**Goal:** Bring stop-start into actual sex with a partner. The most directly applicable behavioral intervention.

---

Today, applied stop-start with your partner.

[PAUSE 2s]

This is the first session where the work moves into actual partnered sex. It's also where most of the previous training pays off in a directly observable way.

[PAUSE 3s]

The setup.

[PAUSE 2s]

You and your partner have already done at least one sensate focus session together. You have a working communication frame. You can talk about what's happening.

[PAUSE 2s]

For this practice, plan a sex session — at a time that works for both of you, with privacy, with no rush. Both of you know in advance that today's session is partly an exercise. That's important. It removes ambiguity.

[PAUSE 3s]

The structure.

[PAUSE 2s]

Begin sex however you normally would. There's nothing prescribed about how to start.

[PAUSE 2s]

When you cross into the seven range — when you feel yourself approaching the edge — call a pause. Your call. Hand on your partner's hip, brief verbal cue, whatever you've agreed on. Stop physical activity completely. Stay in contact with your partner — bodies still close — but stop the motion.

[PAUSE 2s]

Take three slow exhales. Long ones. The same breath you've been training.

[PAUSE 2s]

Wait. Thirty to sixty seconds. Let the arousal level drop back to a four or five.

[PAUSE 2s]

Resume. When you cross seven again, pause again.

[PAUSE 3s]

Do this two or three times during the session before continuing to climax — or stop the session without climax, your call.

[PAUSE 3s]

A few things to know.

[PAUSE 2s]

The first time you do this, both of you may find the pauses awkward. The pause feels longer than it is. Talking during the pause is fine — soft, brief. Holding each other is fine. Anything that keeps you connected without driving arousal back up.

[PAUSE 2s]

You will misjudge timing. You'll think you're at a six and discover you're crossing eight. That's normal. The first few sessions are about calibration. The sensitivity sharpens with practice.

[PAUSE 2s]

You may end up climaxing during a pause despite trying not to. That happens. The reflex sometimes fires from the absence of stimulation. Don't make it a failure narrative — log the data and try again next time.

[PAUSE 3s]

The communication piece.

[PAUSE 2s]

Most partners are surprisingly fine with the pauses, once they're framed in advance. The dread of pauses is mostly in the man's head. Partners often report that the pauses feel like more presence, not less — you're more there, not less there.

[PAUSE 2s]

After the session, talk briefly. What worked? What was awkward? Did the pauses break the flow or deepen it? You're both learning.

[PAUSE 3s]

This is now a practice you can use in any partnered sex going forward. Once a week is plenty for explicit practice. The skill quietly transfers to all partnered sex over time.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow, closing the week.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work today.

# Session 19n — "What you can do alone" (PARTNERED-DECLINED PATH)

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]

[PAUSE 2s]

The arousal scale, monitored internally. The breath, applied silently. The cognitive defusion, executed without anyone noticing. [firmer]

[PAUSE 2s]

Without making it an exercise, without announcing pauses, without naming what you're doing. You can slow down. You can shift positions in a way that briefly reduces stimulation. [PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]

[PAUSE 2s]

[PAUSE 2s]

# Session 20n — "Whether to revisit" (PARTNERED-DECLINED PATH)

**Day:** 20, partnered-declined path
**Duration:** ~7 min
**Type:** Lesson + reflection
**Goal:** Help the user decide whether to bring up the conversation again later, and how to read their relationship for the answer.

---

Today, an open question.

[PAUSE 2s]

Your partner said no two days ago. The question now: do you bring it up again? When? How? Or do you let it rest indefinitely?

[PAUSE 3s]

There's no universal answer. It depends on your relationship and what the no actually was. Let me give you a framework.

[PAUSE 3s]

[firmer]
Don't revisit if:

[PAUSE 2s]

Your partner's no was clearly a "this is settled, please don't bring it up again." Some partners are direct in saying that. If that's what you heard, respect it. Pushing repeats the pressure that produced the no.

[PAUSE 2s]

Or — if revisiting would feel like nagging in the context of your relationship. You know your partner. If they're someone who hears repeated requests as pressure, the second ask is the wrong move.

[PAUSE 2s]

Or — if your relationship is going through a stressful period unrelated to sex. Wait until things have stabilized. Adding sexual-practice requests during external stress usually backfires.

[PAUSE 3s]

[firmer]
Consider revisiting if:

[PAUSE 2s]

Your partner's no had a "not now" quality. They didn't seem opposed in principle, just not ready. After two to four weeks, the question lands fresh, not as repetition.

[PAUSE 2s]

Or — something has changed. You've made visible progress on your own. Your partner has commented positively on something different about you sexually. They've raised the topic themselves. These are signs the openness has shifted.

[PAUSE 2s]

Or — you've found a different framing. Maybe the original conversation centered on practices and exercises, and a different framing — "I've noticed I'm more present lately, and I'd love to do something low-key together" — would land better.

[PAUSE 3s]

If you do revisit, three principles.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
One. Don't reference the previous conversation directly. "I know you said no last time, but…" puts your partner on the defensive. Treat the new conversation as its own thing.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Two. Lower the ask. If you originally asked for sensate focus, this time ask for something smaller. "Would you try a fifteen-minute thing with me on Saturday?" is easier than "Would you do regular practice with me?"

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Three. Mean what you say about it being optional. If you've internally decided that this revisit is your last attempt and you'll be hurt if they say no again, your partner will feel that pressure. Better to revisit only when you can genuinely take another no without it loading the relationship.

[PAUSE 3s]

A more honest possibility worth naming.

[PAUSE 2s]

Some partners will never want to do explicit practice work, and that's a fact about who they are, not a temporary state. If you've revisited once and gotten another no, you have your answer for the medium term. Continue the protocol. Continue the work alone. Let the question rest.

[PAUSE 2s]

This isn't a tragedy. The work transfers. Your sex life can meaningfully improve without your partner ever doing a formal practice with you.

[PAUSE 3s]

For today, a reflection.

[PAUSE 2s]

Without deciding anything yet — what do you actually think your partner's no was? A "not now"? A "not in this framing"? A "settled, please drop it"? Or somewhere else?

[PAUSE 5s]

Sit with what you actually believe, not what you wish were true.

[PAUSE 2s]

That belief, more than any general rule, will tell you whether to revisit and when.
