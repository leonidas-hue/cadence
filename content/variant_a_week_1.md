# Variant A — Week 1: Foundations

**Profile:** Anxiety-Driven Acquired PE
**Week 1 theme:** *Slowing down the system.*
**Total sessions:** 7 (one per day)
**Average duration:** 5–8 minutes
**Voice direction:** Calm, measured, warm but not soft. Think "grounded older brother" rather than "wellness influencer." No vocal fry, no rising intonation at the end of statements. Consistent voice across all sessions — recommend ElevenLabs "Adam" or "Brian" as starting points.

---

## Audio script conventions used in this document

- `[PAUSE 3s]` — silence beats. ElevenLabs respects these literally.
- `[BREATH IN — 4s]` — paced cue, audio guides the breath.
- `[INHALE]` / `[EXHALE]` — short single-breath cues.
- `[soft]` / `[firmer]` — tone shift instructions for voice generation.
- `(stage direction)` — not voiced; for voice talent or AI direction reference.

# Session 4 — "Spectatoring"

**Day:** 4
**Duration:** ~6 min
**Type:** Educational lesson with reflection prompt
**Goal:** Introduce the cognitive distortion of self-monitoring during sex.

---

Today we talk about something that almost every man with performance anxiety does — and almost none of us realize we're doing.

[PAUSE 2s]

Therapists call it spectatoring.

[PAUSE 2s]

It looks like this.

[PAUSE 1s]

You're with a partner. Things are going well.

[PAUSE 1s]

Then a part of your brain — usually somewhere behind your eyes — pulls back and starts watching.

[PAUSE 1s]

Watching the encounter from the outside. Asking questions.

[PAUSE 1s]

Are they enjoying this? Am I close? How long has it been? Is this position the right one? Should I slow down? Are they getting bored?

[PAUSE 2s]

That's spectatoring. And here's the thing — the moment you spectate, two things happen.

[PAUSE 1s]

[firmer]
First, you leave your body. You stop feeling the actual sensations of sex. The pleasure becomes thinner, more abstract. It's harder to enjoy what's happening because you're not really there.

[PAUSE 2s]

Second, your nervous system reads this self-monitoring as a threat.

[PAUSE 1s]

The voice asking "am I doing okay?" is functionally identical to the voice that asked "am I doing okay?" before you gave a public speech, or before a job interview, or before any high-pressure performance.

[PAUSE 1s]

Your body responds with sympathetic arousal. Heart rate up. Muscles tight.

[PAUSE 1s]

And the ejaculation reflex moves closer.

[PAUSE 2s]

The cruel part: spectatoring usually starts because you're worried about finishing too fast. So the very act of trying to monitor your timing — accelerates your timing.

[PAUSE 2s]

So what do you do instead?

[PAUSE 1s]

You bring attention back into the body.

[PAUSE 1s]

Specifically, into sensation. Not into what your partner might be thinking. Not into a mental clock.

[PAUSE 1s]

Into the actual physical experience — what their skin feels like, what your own breath feels like, what your hands are touching.

[PAUSE 2s]

This is a skill. It's a muscle you have to build. And we're going to build it slowly over the next several weeks.

[PAUSE 2s]

For today, just one reflection.

[PAUSE 1s]

Take a moment now and ask yourself: what does spectatoring look like for you?

[PAUSE 1s]

When you've had partnered sex recently, what was the voice in your head saying? What was it asking?

[PAUSE 5s]

Just notice… no judgment. The awareness itself is the first step.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow we return to breath, with a longer practice.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work today.
