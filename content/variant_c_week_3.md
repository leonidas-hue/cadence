# Variant C — Week 3: Stop-Start

**Profile:** Lifelong / Hypotonic PE
**Week 3 theme:** *Awareness training plus retreat training, repeated.*
**Total sessions:** 7
**Average duration:** 5–9 minutes audio + 15–25 min self-directed practice on practice days
**Voice direction:** Same Adam / Bella. Calmer than Variant A's stop-start week — Variant A's user is anxious; Variant C's user is patient and analytical. Lean into that. The voice can be more direct, less reassuring.

**Content philosophy:** Week 3 is the headline behavioral week. The user has built pelvic floor strength foundation; now they apply the most clinically-validated PE technique. Three solo practice sessions across the week, each slightly longer than the last. The biggest pitfall is the user trying to "succeed" at stop-start by crossing a high threshold without slipping — but the actual learning happens in the misjudgments. We frame this directly.

# Session 15 — "First solo stop-start"

**Day:** 15
**Duration:** ~6 min audio for ~15 min self-directed practice
**Type:** Self-directed practice with audio setup
**Goal:** First explicit solo stop-start. Conservative session — climb to six, single hold, single back-off.

[PAUSE 2s]

[PAUSE 3s]

The setup.

[PAUSE 2s]

[PAUSE 2s]

[PAUSE 3s]

[PAUSE 2s]

[PAUSE 2s]

[PAUSE 3s]

[PAUSE 2s]

The skill we're building is sustained time at six and seven without crossing eight, and the only way to build it is repetitions of climbing-and-backing-off in that range.

[PAUSE 2s]

# Session 17 — "Adding breath to the practice"

**Day:** 17
**Duration:** ~6 min audio for ~18 min self-directed practice
**Type:** Self-directed practice
**Goal:** Second solo stop-start session, now with explicit breath integration during the back-off phase.

---

Today, the second practice session. Same structure, with one addition.

[PAUSE 2s]

When you stop and back off, use the breath actively. Specifically, four-eight breathing during the back-off period.

[PAUSE 3s]

Why this matters.

[PAUSE 2s]

The exhale pulls heart rate down. Heart rate is part of the arousal cascade. When heart rate drops, sympathetic activation drops, and arousal level drops faster than it would from absence of stimulation alone.

[PAUSE 2s]

In yesterday's session — the first one — your back-off period probably took thirty to sixty seconds. With active breath, the back-off can drop to twenty to forty seconds. Smaller window of pause. More cycles possible per session. Faster recovery to baseline.

[PAUSE 3s]

The structure of today's session.

[PAUSE 2s]

Set aside about eighteen minutes. Same setup as before — privacy, time, comfortable position.

[PAUSE 2s]

Three minutes of four-eight breath to start.

[PAUSE 2s]

Then begin solo arousal work. Climb to a six.

[PAUSE 2s]

When you stop, immediately begin four-eight breath. Three to five long exhales. Let the breath actively drop you, not just absence of stimulation.

[PAUSE 2s]

When you're back at four, resume. Climb to six again. Stop again. Breathe.

[PAUSE 2s]

Today's target: four cycles instead of three. The breath should make this possible without extending the session length.

[PAUSE 3s]

A specific note.

[PAUSE 2s]

Some men, the first time they try active breathing during back-off, find it hard to do both — read arousal level *and* execute the breath. Attention splits. The breath becomes mechanical and hollow. Or attention drifts toward the breath and away from arousal monitoring.

[PAUSE 2s]

If that happens, here's the order of priority. Breath first. The breath is doing real work — pulling sympathetic down. Arousal monitoring will come back online once the breath is solid. For the first few sessions of breath-integrated stop-start, just do the breath. Don't try to also monitor with precision. The monitoring sharpens with practice.

[PAUSE 3s]

By the third or fourth session of this kind, breath and monitoring will run in parallel automatically. You're building a multi-system skill, and the systems learn to coordinate over reps.

[PAUSE 3s]

Do the practice today, sometime later when you have time. Tomorrow we look at the most common pitfall in this phase.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work today.

# Session 18 — "The most common pitfall"

**Day:** 18
**Duration:** ~6 min
**Type:** Lesson
**Goal:** Address the "I'm getting good at this" trap — when the user starts trying for higher thresholds and crosses too often.

---

Today, a pitfall that shows up around session two or three of stop-start work.

[PAUSE 2s]

You've done one or two sessions. The basic mechanic is starting to make sense. You feel like you have some sense of where the threshold is and how the back-off works.

[PAUSE 2s]

So you push.

[PAUSE 3s]

Specifically, you start trying to climb to seven instead of six, or even seven-and-a-half. You want to feel closer to the edge. You want to test how close you can get and still pull back.

[PAUSE 2s]

This is the natural instinct, and it's the trap.

[PAUSE 3s]

Here's what happens. At seven, the reflex starts pulling you toward eight involuntarily. The sympathetic activation has gained momentum. The breath helps less than at six, because the arousal pathway is steeper at seven. You either pull off cleanly — feels good, you got close — or you cross. And once you start crossing more frequently, the protocol stops being calibration training and becomes a series of edge-failures.

[PAUSE 2s]

Edge-failures don't just feel like sub-par sessions. They reinforce the original wiring. Each time you try to control yourself at seven and lose, your nervous system codes seven-leads-to-eight more strongly. That's the opposite of what we want.

[PAUSE 3s]

The discipline.

[PAUSE 2s]

Stay at six for at least the first ten or twelve sessions of stop-start work. Don't get curious about seven yet. The work at six is what builds the foundation that makes seven safe to attempt later.

[PAUSE 2s]

If a session feels boring because you're "only" at six — that's fine. Boring is the right texture for foundational training. Boredom is the muscle building. Excitement at the edge is the muscle failing.

[PAUSE 3s]

When does seven become appropriate?

[PAUSE 2s]

Around week five or six, when you've done at least eight to ten clean sessions at six and feel reliably comfortable backing off. By then your threshold awareness has sharpened enough that seven is something you can read clearly, and your back-off has gotten fast enough that the small extra distance from seven is recoverable.

[PAUSE 2s]

We'll cover the transition to seven explicitly when we get there.

[PAUSE 3s]

Today, no practice session. Just the awareness — when the practice gets familiar enough that you want to push, that's the moment to consciously stay at six instead.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow, the third practice session.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work today.

[PAUSE 3s]

[PAUSE 2s]

The hold at six is the highest-leverage skill in this whole technique. It's where partnered sex actually happens — at six, sustained, not constantly retreating. # Session 20 — "Between sessions"

**Day:** 20
**Duration:** ~6 min
**Type:** Lesson
**Goal:** Discuss what to do — and what not to do — between formal practice sessions. Address frequency, masturbation outside of practice, and recovery.

---

Today, between-session integration.

[PAUSE 2s]

You've done three formal stop-start sessions this week. The natural questions: how often should this happen, what about masturbation outside the protocol, when does this transition to partnered work.

[PAUSE 3s]

Frequency first.

[PAUSE 2s]

Stop-start practice is a learning task, not a strength training task. The body needs time between sessions to consolidate the calibration. Two to three sessions per week is the sweet spot. More than that, and the calibration gets noisy — each session interferes with the consolidation of the previous one. Less than that, and you forget between sessions.

[PAUSE 2s]

Two to three sessions per week, with at least 48 hours between any two. Continue this rhythm for the next four weeks at minimum.

[PAUSE 3s]

Masturbation outside the protocol.

[PAUSE 2s]

If you masturbate outside of formal stop-start sessions, that's neither encouraged nor discouraged. The protocol doesn't require abstinence between practices. But there's a useful principle.

[PAUSE 2s]

If you masturbate without intent — fast pace, tight grip, racing to finish — you're reinforcing the fast-reflex pattern even while doing protocol work. Solo sessions outside the protocol are practice opportunities, even when they're casual. Try to keep grip moderate and pace varied even when you're not doing a formal session.

[PAUSE 2s]

This isn't about purity. It's just that the nervous system trains constantly, not only during the sessions you've labeled "training." If most of your masturbation reinforces the old pattern, the formal sessions are working against headwind.

[PAUSE 3s]

Partnered work.

[PAUSE 2s]

If you're partnered, you can begin applying stop-start informally during partnered sex from this week onward. You don't need to announce it. You don't need exercises. Just internally use the same skills — read arousal level, slow down or pause when approaching seven, breathe through the back-off.

[PAUSE 2s]

If your partner is open to it, you can also tell them what you're working on at whatever level of detail feels right. Some men find it useful; others don't. There's no right answer.

[PAUSE 2s]

If you're not partnered, this transition happens later — whenever partnered sex happens. The solo work transfers. We've talked about this. The skills don't disappear because they were trained alone.

[PAUSE 3s]

Recovery.

[PAUSE 2s]

If a session goes poorly — you cross over, you can't find six, the breath doesn't work — give yourself an extra rest day. Don't push to redo the session. The next attempt should be at least 72 hours later, not 24.

[PAUSE 2s]

Bad sessions in this phase aren't a sign that the protocol is failing. They're a sign that the system is finding its calibration, which involves overshooting and undershooting before settling.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow we close week three.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work today.
