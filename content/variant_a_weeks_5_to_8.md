# Variant A — Weeks 5–8: Integration

**Theme arc:**
- Week 5 — *Generalizing.* Taking skills out of practice contexts and into life.
- Week 6 — *The hard moments.* What to do when things go wrong.
- Week 7 — *Identity, not technique.* Who you're becoming.
- Week 8 — *Maintenance and the long view.* What this looks like after the program.

**Total sessions:** 28 (7 per week)
**Average duration:** 4–8 minutes
**Voice direction:** Same Adam / Bella. Tone shifts slightly — more conversational, more reflective, less instructional. The user has been with the voice for a month. The voice can be more relaxed.

**Content philosophy:** The second half of the program produces less new material on purpose. The risk in any 8-week protocol is that weeks 5–8 become repetitions of weeks 1–4 with different language, and users disengage. Instead, weeks 5–8 are about *durability* — taking the skills out of training contexts and making them robust in actual life. Several sessions are short framing intros that send the user to the practice library for the day's actual work. This isn't padding — it's the right structure for consolidation.

[PAUSE 2s]

[PAUSE 3s]

[PAUSE 2s]

## Session 37 — "After a bad sex moment"

**Day:** 37 · ~7 min · Lesson + practice

---

The most common scenario. You had partnered sex. It went worse than you wanted. The catastrophic narrative is running.

[PAUSE 3s]

Here's the recovery sequence.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
First — don't make it bigger than it was.

[PAUSE 2s]

Don't apologize five times. Don't give a lengthy explanation. Don't promise things will be different next time. All of these signal "this was a disaster," and your partner will start to read it that way too.

[PAUSE 2s]

What works better: stay in the room. Stay close. Maintain physical contact if it feels right. If you say anything about it, keep it brief and non-catastrophic. Something like — "earlier than I wanted." Not "I'm so sorry that was awful I always do this."

[PAUSE 3s]

[firmer]
Second — don't withdraw afterward.

[PAUSE 2s]

The instinct after a bad sex moment is often to go silent. Get up. Find something to do. Avoid eye contact. The withdrawal is partly shame, partly self-protection.

[PAUSE 2s]

Withdrawal is exactly what makes the moment bigger. It signals to your partner that something serious happened. It also reinforces, internally, that something bad happened.

[PAUSE 2s]

Stay. Talk about something else if you want — anything else. Eat something. Watch something. Be a person in the room.

[PAUSE 3s]

[firmer]
Third — within twenty-four hours, run a recovery practice.

[PAUSE 2s]

The recovery practice is the same daily session you've been doing. Breath. Body awareness. Maybe a short meditation. By "recovery practice" I mean whichever short practice has become your usual one — the breath, the body scan, or both. If you don't have a default, default to extended exhale for five minutes. The point is to *re-anchor* yourself in the protocol after the disruption. Without this, the bad moment becomes a fork in the road — you either keep going with the work, or you start to drift away from it.

[PAUSE 3s]

[firmer]
Fourth — at some point in the next week, have a short conversation with your partner.

[PAUSE 2s]

Not an apology. A check-in. Something like — "I noticed the other night didn't go the way I wanted. I'm working on this stuff. I don't want it to be a big thing between us. How was it for you?"

[PAUSE 2s]

The "how was it for you" is critical. It opens the question without assuming the answer. Most partners, asked this directly, give surprisingly grounded answers. The catastrophic narrative usually doesn't survive contact with the actual partner.

[PAUSE 3s]

The thing about this sequence — it's not glamorous. There's no clever reframe that makes the bad moment feel good. The work is just absorbing it without spiraling. Doing the next thing. Continuing the protocol.

[PAUSE 3s]

[soft]

## Session 39 — "When practice feels pointless"

Today, a more subtle scenario. The one where you haven't quit, but you're starting to feel like the practice is pointless.

[PAUSE 3s]

[PAUSE 2s]

This phase is real and predictable. Most behavioral change protocols hit it. Some men quit at this point — they conclude that the protocol stopped working, when really the work has just shifted from its visible-progress phase to its consolidation phase.

[PAUSE 3s]

The honest answer about this phase is that it's *the work*.

[PAUSE 2s]

The first weeks were learning new skills, which is intrinsically engaging. The middle weeks are practicing them, which is less engaging but builds depth. The later weeks are consolidating them into how you operate, which is least engaging but most durable.

[PAUSE 2s]

If you only stayed for the engaging part, you'd lose most of the value. The unsexy consolidation phase is where the skill becomes yours.

[PAUSE 3s]

A few things to notice if you're in this phase.

[PAUSE 2s]

If the daily practice feels boring, that's actually a good sign. It means the practice has become familiar. Familiar things feel boring. They also feel automatic when you need them.

[PAUSE 2s]

If you can't tell whether you've improved, that's also normal. Improvement at this stage is incremental and easy to miss day-to-day. Compare your current state to *six weeks ago*, not to yesterday. Six weeks ago, did you even know what the arousal scale was? Did you know what it felt like to release your jaw? Did you know what spectatoring was?

[PAUSE 3s]

A specific practice for the boredom phase.

[PAUSE 2s]

Vary the daily session slightly. Same skills, different sequence. Sometimes do breath first then body scan, sometimes the reverse. Try four-eight breathing if you usually do four-six. Try the pre-session prep audio even when there's no session ahead.

[PAUSE 2s]

The variation isn't strictly necessary — repetition is the point — but it helps with engagement. Engagement helps with consistency. Consistency is what produces the changes you'll see at the end.

## Session 40 — "When you're worse, not better"

**Day:** 40 · ~7 min · Lesson

---

Today, the rarest scenario. The one where you've been doing the work, you're six weeks in, and partnered sex feels worse than it did before you started.

[PAUSE 3s]

This happens to a small percentage of users. Not most. But enough that it deserves a session.

[PAUSE 3s]

Here's what's usually going on when it happens.

[PAUSE 2s]

The work has made you more aware of what's happening internally during sex. Three months ago, when sex went poorly, you noticed only the gross outcome — finished too fast. Now you're noticing the entire arc — the spectatoring at minute two, the breath disappearing at minute three, the catastrophic narrative starting at minute five.

[PAUSE 2s]

The granular awareness creates a feeling of *more is going wrong*. Actually less is going wrong — you just see more of it.

[PAUSE 3s]

This is a paradox of skill development. Beginners often feel like they're doing fine because they don't see what's wrong. As skill develops, the sense of imperfection sharpens. This shows up everywhere — in music, in sports, in writing, in sex.

[PAUSE 3s]

There's also a second possibility, less common but real. Some men, partway through the protocol, start anticipating the practice during sex itself. *Am I breathing? Am I checking the scale? Am I being present?* The monitoring becomes its own form of spectatoring.

[PAUSE 2s]

If that's you, the move is to back off the explicit monitoring. The skills you've trained should be operating in the background by now. If you're consciously running them during sex, something has gotten over-engineered.

[PAUSE 2s]

Drop the monitoring. Trust that what you've practiced is there. Let your attention go to your partner, the sensation, the moment. The skills do their work without you watching them work.

[PAUSE 3s]

A third possibility, rarer still. The work has surfaced something that needs more than this protocol can offer. Significant relationship issues, deeper trauma history, mental health concerns that PE was a smaller piece of.

[PAUSE 2s]

If you've noticed real distress that goes beyond performance anxiety, this app can't be the only thing you're doing. Talk to a therapist. Specifically, look for a sex therapist or a CBT therapist with experience in sexual concerns.

[PAUSE 2s]

Cadence and good therapy are complementary, not in conflict.

## Session 41 — "The hardest skill"

**Day:** 41 · ~6 min · Lesson

---

Today, the skill that runs underneath all the others.

[PAUSE 2s]

Self-compassion under failure.

[PAUSE 3s]

I waited until week six to name this directly because the language is overused — every wellness program talks about self-compassion, and the words have lost their meaning through repetition.

[PAUSE 2s]

So let me be specific. Self-compassion in this context is one thing only — when something goes wrong, talking to yourself the way you'd talk to a close friend in the same situation.

[PAUSE 3s]

Imagine your closest male friend tells you he had bad sex with his partner. Finished faster than he wanted. Felt awful afterward.

[PAUSE 2s]

What would you actually say to him?

[PAUSE 3s]

Almost certainly not — *yeah man, that's pathetic, what's wrong with you, your partner must be so disappointed.*

[PAUSE 2s]

You'd probably say something like — *that happens to a lot of guys. Don't make a bigger deal of it than it is. It doesn't mean what your brain is telling you it means.*

[PAUSE 3s]

Now notice what you actually say to yourself when the same thing happens. The internal voice tends to be much harsher than the voice you'd use with a friend. Often dramatically harsher.

[PAUSE 3s]

That gap — between how you'd treat a friend and how you treat yourself — is the gap self-compassion closes.

[PAUSE 2s]

The skill is not feeling good after a setback. The skill is talking to yourself the way you'd talk to someone you cared about.

[PAUSE 3s]

This sounds soft. It isn't. The harsh internal voice is the engine that turns small setbacks into spirals. The friend-voice is what lets you have a bad moment, absorb it, and continue with the work.

[PAUSE 3s]

Practice this in low-stakes settings first. The next time you make a small mistake — drop something, forget something, send a typo — notice your internal reaction. Is it disproportionately harsh? Substitute the friend-voice. *Not a big deal. Move on.*

[PAUSE 2s]

When the harsh voice runs after a sex moment, you'll have already built the substitute. It comes more easily.
