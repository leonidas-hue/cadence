# Variant B — Week 1: Releasing the Floor

**Profile:** Hypertonic Pelvic Floor
**Week 1 theme:** *Letting go of what's been gripping for years.*
**Total sessions:** 7 (one per day, with same Day 1 → Day 2 immediate-unlock as Variant A)
**Average duration:** 5–9 minutes
**Voice direction:** Same Adam / Bella voice. Even softer than Variant A. The user with a hypertonic floor is often a high-strung person — gripping is their nervous-system pattern. The voice itself models the parasympathetic state we're teaching. Slow tempo, generous pauses, warm but not cheerful.

**Content philosophy:** This variant has more responsibility than the others. Standard PE advice — "do Kegels, stop-and-start, squeeze technique" — actively harms this user's profile. We have to be clear about what we're *not* doing, recommend pelvic floor physiotherapy explicitly, and make the user feel *safe* in the protocol. The biggest risk is a hypertonic user feeling skeptical, googling "Kegels for PE," and ending up worse off.

# Session 2 — "Diaphragmatic breathing"

**Day:** 2
**Duration:** ~8 min
**Type:** Practice (foundational breath)
**Goal:** Teach belly breathing. The diaphragm and pelvic floor move together — when one drops, the other drops. Belly breathing is therefore a direct pelvic floor release tool.

---

Today we learn the foundation skill for your protocol. Diaphragmatic breathing.

[PAUSE 2s]

Most people don't actually breathe with their diaphragm. They breathe with their chest and shoulders. You can see this — watch yourself in a mirror taking a breath. If your shoulders rise and your chest expands forward, you're chest-breathing.

[PAUSE 2s]

Diaphragmatic breathing is different. The diaphragm is a dome-shaped muscle under your lungs. When you breathe in deeply through the diaphragm, it flattens and pushes down — which makes the belly expand outward. When you exhale, the diaphragm rises back up and the belly falls.

[PAUSE 3s]

Here's why this matters for your profile.

[PAUSE 2s]

The diaphragm and the pelvic floor are connected. They work as a pair — when the diaphragm drops on the inhale, the pelvic floor drops with it. When the diaphragm rises on the exhale, the pelvic floor lifts.

[PAUSE 2s]

In a chronically tense pelvic floor, this coordination is broken. The pelvic floor stays gripped during the inhale instead of dropping. So one of the most direct things we can do — without ever touching the pelvic floor consciously — is restore the diaphragm's natural movement. The floor follows.

[PAUSE 3s]

Let's practice.

[PAUSE 2s]

Lie on your back. Knees bent, feet flat on the floor. Or in bed if you prefer. The position matters — lying down lets gravity do part of the work for you.

[PAUSE 4s]

Place one hand on your chest. Place the other hand on your belly, just below the navel.

[PAUSE 3s]

Take a normal breath. Don't change anything yet. Just notice — which hand moves more? The chest hand or the belly hand?

[PAUSE 5s]

For most people in your profile, the chest hand moves more. We're going to flip that.

[PAUSE 3s]

Now, the practice.

[PAUSE 2s]

Inhale slowly through the nose. As you inhale, imagine you're filling your belly with air, not your chest. The belly hand should rise. The chest hand should stay almost still.

[PAUSE 2s]

Try one.

[PAUSE 1s]

[BREATH IN — 5s]
Exhale, slow.
[BREATH OUT — 6s]

[PAUSE 3s]

Don't worry if it didn't feel right. Most people need several tries to find the pattern.

[PAUSE 2s]

A trick: imagine your belly is a balloon. The inhale fills the balloon. The exhale empties it.

[PAUSE 2s]

Let's do five together.

[PAUSE 1s]

[soft]
In, fill the belly.
[BREATH IN — 5s]
Out, empty.
[BREATH OUT — 6s]

[soft]
In, belly rises.
[BREATH IN — 5s]
Out, falls.
[BREATH OUT — 6s]

[soft]
In, soft.
[BREATH IN — 5s]
Out, slow.
[BREATH OUT — 6s]

[soft]
In.
[BREATH IN — 5s]
Out.
[BREATH OUT — 6s]

[soft]
In.
[BREATH IN — 5s]
Out.
[BREATH OUT — 6s]

[PAUSE 3s]

Continue this on your own for two minutes. Hand on the belly, hand on the chest. Belly rises, belly falls. If your shoulders sneak up, just notice and reset.

[PAUSE 2s]

Begin.

[PAUSE 120s]

[soft]
Come back when you're ready.

[PAUSE 3s]

Notice. Many men with your profile feel something subtle here — a feeling of slight release in the lower abdomen or pelvis after a few minutes of belly breathing. That's the diaphragm dropping. And underneath, the pelvic floor releasing along with it.

[PAUSE 2s]

This is the foundation of your protocol. Practice it any time during the day — at your desk, lying in bed before sleep, in the car. The more your body remembers this pattern, the more your pelvic floor remembers how to drop.

[PAUSE 2s]

# Session 3 — "Reverse Kegels"

**Day:** 3
**Duration:** ~9 min
**Type:** Practice (PFM release)
**Goal:** Teach reverse Kegels. The user has likely never consciously dropped their pelvic floor. This session is the first time they meet this skill directly.

---

Today, the reverse Kegel.

[PAUSE 2s]

This is the most important physical skill in your protocol. It's the conscious version of what diaphragmatic breathing does indirectly — releasing and dropping the pelvic floor.

[PAUSE 2s]

It's the opposite of a Kegel. A Kegel pulls the pelvic floor up and in. A reverse Kegel pushes it down and out.

[PAUSE 2s]

Most men in your profile have never done this consciously. Some men will find it strange. Some will find it surprisingly familiar — because it's the same motion the body uses for urination, or for the start of a bowel movement. Don't actually do either now. We just need the muscle memory.

[PAUSE 3s]

Find a comfortable position. Lying on your back with knees bent works best.

[PAUSE 4s]

Three breaths first. Belly breathing. Like yesterday.

[PAUSE 1s]

[soft]
In, belly rises.
[BREATH IN — 5s]
Out.
[BREATH OUT — 6s]

[soft]
In.
[BREATH IN — 5s]
Out.
[BREATH OUT — 6s]

[soft]
In.
[BREATH IN — 5s]
Out.
[BREATH OUT — 6s]

[PAUSE 3s]

Now the reverse Kegel.

[PAUSE 2s]

On the next inhale, as the belly rises, also let the pelvic floor drop downward. Imagine pushing down and out — gently. As if you were just starting to urinate, but without actually doing it.

[PAUSE 2s]

The motion should feel like a gentle bulging-out. Not straining. Not hard pushing. Just a soft release downward.

[PAUSE 3s]

Let's try.

[PAUSE 1s]

[soft]

[BREATH OUT — 6s]

[PAUSE 3s]

[soft]

Inhale. Drop the floor.
[BREATH IN — 5s]
Hold.
[PAUSE 1s]
Exhale. Soft.
[BREATH OUT — 6s]

[PAUSE 3s]

Now, your turn. Continue the pattern. Inhale-belly-floor-drops, exhale-neutral. Eight more times. Slow.

[PAUSE 120s]

[soft]

[PAUSE 3s]

Some honest notes about this practice.

[PAUSE 2s]

The first few times, you may not feel sure you're doing it right. That's normal. The pelvic floor is a deep muscle that most people never think about consciously. The motion is subtle.

[PAUSE 2s]

[PAUSE 2s]

[PAUSE 2s]

# Session 4 — "Hip mobility"

**Day:** 4
**Duration:** ~9 min
**Type:** Practice (movement)
**Goal:** Three hip mobility positions that release pelvic floor tension indirectly. The user holds each position while breathing diaphragmatically.

---

Today, hip mobility.

[PAUSE 2s]

Tight hips and a tense pelvic floor almost always come together. The same nervous system pattern that grips the floor also grips the hip flexors, the inner thighs, the deep stabilizers around the pelvis. Loosening the hips loosens the floor.

[PAUSE 2s]

We'll do three positions today. Each one is held for two minutes while you do diaphragmatic breathing. Don't push for depth. The point is the breath in the position, not the stretch itself.

[PAUSE 3s]

You'll need a bit of floor space. A yoga mat is nice but not necessary.

[PAUSE 4s]

[firmer]
Position one. Child's pose with wide knees.

[PAUSE 2s]

Kneel on the floor. Bring your big toes together behind you. Spread your knees wide — wider than your hips. Now sit back so your butt rests on or near your heels, and fold forward, dropping your chest toward the floor between your knees.

[PAUSE 2s]

Arms extended forward, or back along your sides — whichever feels better.

[PAUSE 4s]

If your hips don't get all the way to your heels, that's fine. Use a folded blanket or pillow under your butt for support.

[PAUSE 5s]

Settled? Good.

[PAUSE 2s]

Two minutes here. Belly breathing the entire time. Notice the breath moving into your back and sides — in this position, you can really feel it.

[PAUSE 2s]

Begin.

[PAUSE 120s]

[soft]

[PAUSE 5s]

[firmer]
Position two. Happy baby.

[PAUSE 2s]

[PAUSE 2s]

[PAUSE 3s]

In.
[BREATH IN — 5s]

[BREATH OUT — 6s]

# Session 6 — "The unconscious clench"

**Day:** 6
**Duration:** ~6 min
**Type:** Lesson + practice
**Goal:** Teach the user to notice when they're gripping the pelvic floor during ordinary daily life. Gripping during ordinary life = repetition of the wrong pattern thousands of times per day.

---

Today, something subtle but important.

[PAUSE 2s]

Most men with your profile clench the pelvic floor unconsciously throughout the day. Walking. Sitting at a desk. Driving. While anxious. While focused. While doing nothing in particular.

[PAUSE 2s]

You wouldn't think this is a big deal. But each unconscious clench is a small repetition of the exact pattern we're trying to undo. Hundreds of times a day, your nervous system practices gripping. The reverse Kegel work is a few minutes a day. The unconscious clench is happening for hours.

[PAUSE 3s]

Until you start noticing it, you can't change it.

[PAUSE 2s]

Here's the practice. For the rest of today, set a reminder on your phone — every 90 minutes if you can. When the reminder goes off, do one thing: check in with your pelvic floor.

[PAUSE 2s]

Is it gripping? Or is it soft?

[PAUSE 3s]

Most of the time, when you start noticing, you'll find it gripping. That's the data. Once you've noticed, do one slow breath — belly breath, long exhale — and let the floor drop. That's it. Five seconds of practice.

[PAUSE 3s]

Let's try the check-in now.

[PAUSE 3s]

Take a moment. Don't change your position. Just notice.

[PAUSE 2s]

What's happening at your pelvic floor right now?

[PAUSE 6s]

If there's any grip, do one belly breath — long exhale — and let it release.

[PAUSE 1s]

[soft]

[BREATH IN — 5s]

[BREATH OUT — 7s]

[PAUSE 1s]

[PAUSE 2s]

[PAUSE 2s]
