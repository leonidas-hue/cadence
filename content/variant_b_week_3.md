# Variant B — Week 3: The Cognitive Layer

**Profile:** Hypertonic Pelvic Floor PE
**Week 3 theme:** *The patterns of mind that maintain the patterns of body.*
**Total sessions:** 7
**Average duration:** 6–8 minutes
**Voice direction:** Same Adam / Bella. Continued slow and unhurried. The cognitive work this week could land as harsh if delivered too directly — keep the tone gentle.

**Content philosophy:** Most hypertonic users have anxiety or over-control patterns that maintain the chronic tension. Without addressing the cognitive layer, the physical work counteracts the same generative pattern week after week. Week 3 is CBT-flavored content specific to chronic-tension profiles — vigilance, control, holding-it-together-ness. It's also the week we introduce the arousal scale concept, but only conceptually — actual stop-start application waits until week 4 because the back-off-as-clenching failure mode needs careful setup.

# Session 16 — "Vigilance and the floor"

**Day:** 16
**Duration:** ~7 min
**Type:** Lesson + reflection
**Goal:** Address the most common hypertonic-maintenance pattern — chronic vigilance.

---

Today, vigilance.

[PAUSE 3s]

Most men with hypertonic profiles have a vigilant nervous system. By "vigilant" I mean: it's running with a higher baseline of alertness than is needed for ordinary safety. It's scanning, monitoring, ready for things to go wrong, even when nothing's going wrong.

[PAUSE 2s]

Some signs of this.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Difficulty fully relaxing. Even in safe environments, even when nothing's happening, there's a low-grade buzz of background alertness.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Easily startled. Loud noises, unexpected movements, surprising news lands harder than for most people.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Sleep that doesn't fully reset. You wake up not as rested as you'd expect, or you wake easily during the night.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Trouble being alone with your thoughts. Idle moments fill quickly with worry, planning, or task-checking. Pure rest is uncomfortable.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
A sense that you have to keep an eye on things.

[PAUSE 3s]

If two or three of these resonate, vigilance is likely a baseline state for you.

[PAUSE 2s]

A note before we go on. These markers don't define a disorder, and having several doesn't mean anything is wrong with you. They're patterns your nervous system has settled into, often for good reasons given what your life has involved — work demands, family responsibilities, past stress, the general shape of being a person who handles things. The protocol works with the patterns, not against you.

[PAUSE 3s]

The connection to the floor.

[PAUSE 2s]

The pelvic floor is part of the body's defensive readiness. When the nervous system is scanning, the floor stays slightly engaged — the same way the jaw stays slightly clenched, the shoulders stay slightly raised, the breath stays slightly shallow. They're all expressions of the same underlying state.

[PAUSE 2s]

Releasing the floor while leaving the vigilance untouched is partial work. The vigilance keeps producing tension faster than the practice can release it.

[PAUSE 3s]

A specific practice for this.

[PAUSE 2s]

Three to five times a day, do a brief check-in. Not the body scan — something simpler. Just ask yourself: am I scanning right now?

[PAUSE 2s]

If the answer is yes, take three slow breaths and deliberately stop scanning for thirty seconds. Just stop. Whatever your eyes are doing, soften them. Whatever your mind is monitoring, let it pause. Whatever your body is preparing for, let it set down.

[PAUSE 3s]

Most vigilance is automatic and below conscious awareness. The check-in is a way to bring it into awareness so you can interrupt it, briefly. Each interruption is small but they accumulate.

[PAUSE 3s]

A reflection for today.

[PAUSE 2s]

Where in your life is the vigilance most active? Work? Relationships? Finances? Health? The future generally?

[PAUSE 5s]

Whatever you named — that's the territory the vigilance is operating in. Knowing it specifically is more useful than treating "anxiety" as a vague background fact.

# Session 17 — "Hypercontrol"

**Day:** 17
**Duration:** ~7 min
**Type:** Lesson + reflection
**Goal:** Address the hypercontrol pattern. How the desire to control everything maintains tension everywhere.

---

Today, hypercontrol.

[PAUSE 3s]

Vigilance and hypercontrol are siblings. Vigilance is watchful waiting; hypercontrol is the action that follows from it. If something might go wrong, control everything you can control to prevent it.

[PAUSE 2s]

Some markers.

[PAUSE 2s]

You like things organized. Schedules, environments, plans. Disorder agitates you.

[PAUSE 2s]

You're often the one in your group or relationship who handles logistics. Other people defer to you because you'll get it right.

[PAUSE 2s]

Letting others do things for you is hard. You'd rather do it yourself than wait for someone else's version.

[PAUSE 2s]

Spontaneity is uncomfortable in larger doses. A weekend without plans is more anxiety-inducing than relaxing.

[PAUSE 2s]

When something goes wrong, your first instinct is to figure out how to prevent it next time, even when prevention would be costly or impossible.

[PAUSE 3s]

If that resonates — hypercontrol is part of how you operate.

[PAUSE 3s]

The connection to the floor.

[PAUSE 2s]

The pelvic floor is what closes off the bottom of the torso. In the body's metaphor system, the floor is what holds everything in place. For someone whose default is "everything held in place," the floor is participating literally in the operating system.

[PAUSE 2s]

Releasing the floor while keeping the hypercontrol pattern intact is hard. The floor is doing a job — symbolically, if not mechanically — and the job persists.

[PAUSE 3s]

What to do with this.

[PAUSE 2s]

I'm not going to suggest you become someone who lets things fall apart. The hypercontrol pattern is also responsible for things in your life that work — for your reliability, for your competence, for the structure that supports the people around you.

[PAUSE 2s]

What I'll suggest is a small experiment. Find one area of your life where the control isn't actually load-bearing. Something where you've been controlling not because outcomes require it, but because controlling is your default.

[PAUSE 2s]

Examples. The exact way the dishwasher gets loaded. The specific brand of toothpaste. Whether the laundry gets folded a particular way. The order in which weekend errands happen. The temperature in a room when someone else has set it.

[PAUSE 3s]

For one week — this week — pick one of these and deliberately not control it. Let your partner load the dishwasher their way. Use the toothpaste they bought. Don't refold the laundry. Don't reorder the errands.

[PAUSE 2s]

If you live alone, the examples translate. Let a takeout order arrive in the wrong bag without escalating it. Use a different brand than your usual. Take a different route home from work. Leave dishes overnight without organizing them. The experiment is the same; the targets are different.

[PAUSE 2s]

Notice what happens internally. Specifically — notice if you feel any pelvic floor reaction when the control is released somewhere else.

[PAUSE 3s]

Some men, doing this experiment, notice that letting go of small unnecessary control produces a felt softening in the floor and the abdomen. Others notice nothing. Both are useful data.

[PAUSE 3s]

This isn't a permanent ask. It's a one-week experiment to see what's load-bearing and what isn't.

# Session 18 — "The conversation with anxiety"

**Day:** 18
**Duration:** ~8 min
**Type:** Lesson
**Goal:** A specific reframing approach for anxious thoughts that maintain tension.

---

Today, how to relate to the anxious thoughts that maintain the tension.

[PAUSE 3s]

If vigilance is the watchful state and hypercontrol is the action, anxious thoughts are the running commentary that produces both. Things like — *what if this goes wrong, I should have planned for that, I can't let that happen, this needs to be handled.*

[PAUSE 2s]

These thoughts run continuously for many men with your profile. They're so familiar that you don't notice them as thoughts — they feel like just-the-way-things-are.

[PAUSE 3s]

The standard advice for anxious thoughts is to argue with them, replace them, or push them away. None of these works well.

[PAUSE 2s]

Arguing engages the anxious thought, gives it more weight. Replacement is exhausting and doesn't last. Pushing away is what anxious people have been trying to do, often for years; if it worked, you'd already be free.

[PAUSE 3s]

A different approach.

[PAUSE 2s]

Don't argue. Don't replace. Don't push away. Just notice.

[PAUSE 3s]

Specifically — when an anxious thought shows up, name it briefly. *Worrying about money. Planning for tomorrow. What-iffing about my partner.*

[PAUSE 2s]

The naming creates a small gap between you and the thought. Without the gap, the thought is you — you are the worry, you are the planning. With the gap, the thought is something passing through you. Different relationship.

[PAUSE 3s]

After naming, return your attention to something physical. Your breath. Your feet on the floor. The texture of whatever's in your hand. A specific sound in the room.

[PAUSE 2s]

The thought stays — you're not removing it. But it's now in the background while attention is on the body. The body, given attention, releases. The pelvic floor, given attention through breath, releases.

[PAUSE 3s]

A few things to know about this practice.

[PAUSE 2s]

The thoughts will keep coming. The skill is the noticing and returning, not the elimination of thoughts. Some days you'll notice and return five hundred times. Other days twenty. Both are practice.

[PAUSE 3s]

Some thoughts are more sticky than others. Money worries, health worries, relationship worries — these stick harder than what to make for dinner. Sticky thoughts may need more cycles of notice-return before they let go.

[PAUSE 3s]

For some men with significant anxiety, this practice alone isn't enough. If your anxiety is intrusive — interfering with sleep, work, relationships, daily function — the protocol can help with the body's expression of it but won't fully resolve the underlying issue. Therapy is the appropriate adjunct. Specifically CBT or ACT — the approaches that build on this kind of noticing-returning practice.

[PAUSE 3s]

Do this — the notice-name-return practice — three to five times today. In the moments anxious thoughts come up. Brief, no agenda, just the practice.

# Session 20 — "The arousal scale"

**Day:** 20
**Duration:** ~7 min
**Type:** Lesson
**Goal:** Introduce the arousal scale conceptually. For Variant B, the scale matters but the application requires careful framing — to be done in week 4, not yet.

---

Today, a vocabulary that becomes important next week.

[PAUSE 2s]

The arousal scale.

[PAUSE 3s]

Sex therapists have used a one-to-ten scale for decades because it gives men a vocabulary for the gradient of arousal. Most men have never thought about arousal as a measurable thing. It's just there or not there. The scale changes that.

[PAUSE 3s]

The scale.

[PAUSE 2s]

One is no arousal. Walking down the street.

[PAUSE 1s]

Three is mild interest. Mental engagement, maybe early physical response.

[PAUSE 1s]

Five is solidly aroused. Erection, body engaged.

[PAUSE 1s]

Seven is significantly aroused. Approaching the edge.

[PAUSE 1s]

Eight is the warning zone. Without intervention, you finish in the next minute or two.

[PAUSE 1s]

Nine is point of no return. The reflex is firing.

[PAUSE 1s]

Ten is ejaculation.

[PAUSE 3s]

Why this matters for your profile specifically.

[PAUSE 2s]

There's a specific failure mode for hypertonic users that I need to name now, before we apply the scale next week.

[PAUSE 3s]

The conventional wisdom — and what other men with PE often do — is that when you approach the edge during sex, you should *clench* the pelvic floor to delay ejaculation. Some online resources actually recommend this explicitly. Tighten the floor, hold the breath, brace.

[PAUSE 2s]

For your profile, this is exactly wrong. Clenching the floor at high arousal is doing the same thing your floor has been doing chronically — gripping. The clench-to-delay strategy might briefly delay ejaculation but it reinforces the hypertonic pattern. Worse, it often produces the opposite of intended outcomes — the chronically tight floor, when further tensed at high arousal, can actually trigger faster ejaculation, not slower.

[PAUSE 3s]

[firmer]
The Variant B approach to arousal management is the opposite. Release, not clench. Open, not hold.

[PAUSE 2s]

When you approach seven, the move is to relax the floor — actively reverse-Kegel the floor downward into release. Combined with slow exhale, this reduces arousal without engaging the gripping pattern.

[PAUSE 2s]

This is fundamentally different from what other men with PE are taught. It's specific to your profile.

[PAUSE 3s]

We'll formalize this in week four with practice sessions. For today, just the awareness — when we get to stop-start work, you'll be doing it differently than the standard approach.
