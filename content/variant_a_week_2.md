# Session 9 — "The arousal scale"

**Day:** 9
**Duration:** ~7 min
**Type:** Lesson + reflection
**Goal:** Teach the 1–10 arousal scale framework. (Same conceptual content as Variant C Day 5, adapted slightly for A's framing — the user here is anxiety-driven and we emphasize the awareness-during-arousal angle.)

---

Today we install a vocabulary you'll use from now on.

[PAUSE 2s]

The arousal scale. One to ten.

[PAUSE 3s]

Most men have never thought about arousal as a measurable thing. It's just there or not there. Sex therapists have used a one-to-ten scale for decades because giving people a vocabulary for the gradient changes how they experience it.

[PAUSE 3s]

Let me run through the scale.

[PAUSE 2s]

One is no arousal at all. Walking down the street. Reading a book.

[PAUSE 1s]

Three is mild interest. The early stages. Mental engagement, maybe some early physical response.

[PAUSE 1s]

Five is solidly aroused. Erection, body engaged. Far from finishing.

[PAUSE 1s]

Seven is significantly aroused. Approaching the edge. Still controllable.

[PAUSE 1s]

Eight is the warning zone. You're close. Without intervention, you finish in the next minute or two.

[PAUSE 1s]

Nine is point of no return. The reflex is firing. You finish in seconds.

[PAUSE 1s]

Ten is ejaculation.

[PAUSE 3s]

Why this matters for your profile.

[PAUSE 2s]

Performance-anxiety-driven PE has a specific signature. Most men with your profile go from arousal level three to level eight in a span of seconds, often without consciously registering five, six, or seven happening at all. The anxiety creates a kind of attentional tunneling — you skip the middle of the range entirely.

[PAUSE 2s]

The skill we're building is the ability to *spend time* in the five-to-seven range. Notice it. Stay with it. Use breath and body awareness while you're there.

[PAUSE 3s]

This is harder than it sounds. Anxiety pulls attention away from the body and into the head — and the head, when activated during sex, accelerates everything. So the skill is double — staying in the body, and staying aware enough to read the level.

[PAUSE 2s]

The reading-the-level part is what we practice this week. Solo. With time. With nothing depending on the outcome.

[PAUSE 3s]

For today, a reflection.

[PAUSE 2s]

Think back to the last few times you had partnered sex or did solo work. Try to map the experience to this scale.

[PAUSE 2s]

Where did you spend most of your time? At three? Briefly at five? Did you skip from four to nine? Did you ever notice eight before it became nine?

[PAUSE 5s]

Most men, the first time they do this exercise, realize they've never thought about it this way. Most of the experience was unmonitored. Awareness blanket-fogged.

[PAUSE 2s]

That's the starting point. We turn awareness on, deliberately, over the next several weeks.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow, your first solo practice with the scale in mind.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work today.

# Session 10 — "First solo practice"

**Day:** 10
**Duration:** ~6 min (lesson) + 15–20 min self-directed practice
**Type:** Self-directed practice with audio guidance
**Goal:** First explicit solo practice. The audio sets up the structure; the user does the practice on their own time.

---

Today's session is short audio, longer self-directed practice afterward.

[PAUSE 2s]

The instruction is for a solo practice you'll do later today, in private, with adequate time. Listen now, then do the practice when you can.

[PAUSE 3s]

The structure.

[PAUSE 2s]

Set aside fifteen to twenty minutes. Phone away. No clock-watching.

[PAUSE 2s]

Start with three minutes of extended exhale breathing. Same as week one. Lying down or sitting comfortably. This brings the nervous system into parasympathetic state.

[PAUSE 3s]

Then begin solo arousal work — masturbation. With one specific instruction: *take your time*.

[PAUSE 2s]

Most men, especially with your profile, masturbate quickly. They get to climax in under five minutes, sometimes much less. Today, you're going to deliberately go slowly.

[PAUSE 3s]

Light grip. Slow pace. Generous pauses. Don't try to climb the arousal scale. Try to stay in the lower part — three to five — for the first ten minutes.

[PAUSE 3s]

While you're doing this, your job is to internally check the scale. Where am I? Three? Five?

[PAUSE 2s]

When you notice you're climbing — say, you cross from five to six — slow down further. Or pause. Take a breath. Long exhale. Notice the level drop slightly.

[PAUSE 3s]

This is the entire skill. Notice. Slow down. Breathe. Notice again.

[PAUSE 3s]

Some honest expectations.

[PAUSE 2s]

The first time you try this, you may find it surprisingly hard. Once you cross five, the impulse to keep going gets strong. Slowing down — especially pausing entirely — feels counterintuitive. You may also lose your erection during a pause. That's fine. Erection comes back when stimulation resumes. Let the experience happen.

[PAUSE 2s]

You may climb to seven or eight despite trying not to. That's also fine. The data is what we want — where in the experience did awareness slip? When did the breath disappear? Where did pace pick up without you noticing?

[PAUSE 3s]

You can finish the session, or not. Both are fine. The point isn't ejaculation or non-ejaculation. The point is the awareness practice during arousal.

[PAUSE 3s]

If you're partnered and you'd feel weird doing this with your partner around, do it when they're out. This is private practice, not relationship work yet.

[PAUSE 3s]

After the session, take thirty seconds. Mentally note: where did your awareness work well? Where did it slip? At what level did the breath disappear? You don't need to write anything down — just notice.

[PAUSE 3s]

That's the practice. Listen to this session as many times as you need to before you do the work.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow, sensate focus.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work.

# Session 11 — "Sensate focus, solo"

**Day:** 11
**Duration:** ~8 min (audio) for a 15-min self-directed practice
**Type:** Practice (sensate focus, solo version)
**Goal:** Introduce the sensate focus framework. Solo version this week; partner version becomes available in week 3 if applicable.

---

Today, sensate focus.

[PAUSE 2s]

Sensate focus was developed in the 1960s by Masters and Johnson and remains the gold standard for performance-anxiety-driven sexual issues. It sounds simple. It's not.

[PAUSE 2s]

The core principle: take the goal out. Make the experience about pure sensation, with no end-state to reach.

[PAUSE 3s]

The original version was a partnered exercise. Today's solo version adapts the same principle.

[PAUSE 3s]

Here's how it works.

[PAUSE 2s]

Set aside fifteen minutes. Privacy. No interruptions.

[PAUSE 3s]

Strip down to whatever feels comfortable. Underwear is fine. Naked is fine. Whatever lets you focus on physical sensation.

[PAUSE 3s]

Sit or lie comfortably. Hands free.

[PAUSE 3s]

For the next fifteen minutes, your only task is to touch your own body — slowly, with attention — and pay attention to what you feel. The whole body. Not the genitals. Not for now.

[PAUSE 3s]

Your forearms. Your chest. Your stomach. Your thighs. Your face. Your scalp.

[PAUSE 2s]

Some areas you've touched ten thousand times without ever really paying attention to. Today you pay attention.

[PAUSE 3s]

The instruction: vary the touch. Light. Firm. Slow drag. Quick brush. Notice which sensations are pleasant, which are neutral, which are surprisingly intense, which are dull.

[PAUSE 3s]

Three rules.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
One. No genital touching during this exercise. Not because genital sensation is bad, but because we're training the rest of the body to register pleasure on its own merits.

[PAUSE 2s]

Most men have collapsed sensual sensation entirely into genital sensation. Sensate focus reopens the wider field.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Two. No goal. You're not trying to get aroused. You're not trying to *not* get aroused. Whatever happens with arousal level, fine. Just keep paying attention to the sensation, not the agenda.

[PAUSE 2s]

[firmer]
Three. Stay in the body, not the head. If you find yourself thinking about your shopping list — that's fine, no big deal — just gently bring attention back to whatever you're touching right now. Skin, temperature, texture.

[PAUSE 3s]

Some honest expectations.

[PAUSE 2s]

This will probably feel weird the first time. Touching yourself slowly with no goal feels strange to most adults. The mind tries to make it into something — make it sexual, make it productive, make it efficient. Notice that pull, and stay with the sensation.

[PAUSE 2s]

You may find some areas of your body feel surprisingly responsive — places you didn't know were sensitive. You may find some areas feel numb, less alive than expected. Both are normal. Both are useful information.

[PAUSE 3s]

After fifteen minutes, stop. Take a few breaths. Notice how you feel.

[PAUSE 3s]

Many men report that the first sensate focus session shifts something — a slight loosening of how they relate to physical sensation. Less goal-bound. Less efficient. More open.

[PAUSE 2s]

Some report nothing. That's also fine. Sensate focus rewards repetition.

[PAUSE 3s]

Repeat this practice, either today or another day this week. It's optional from here on, but the more you do it, the more the body opens to non-genital sensation — which becomes the foundation for staying present during partnered sex.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow, intentional stop-start.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work today.

# Session 12 — "Solo stop-start"

**Day:** 12
**Duration:** ~6 min (audio) for 15–20 min self-directed practice
**Type:** Practice (stop-start, solo)
**Goal:** First explicit solo stop-start practice. Combines the arousal scale, breath, and the slow-down impulse from Day 10.

---

Today, your first deliberate solo stop-start practice.

[PAUSE 2s]

Same setup as day three of this week. Fifteen to twenty minutes. Privacy. Time.

[PAUSE 3s]

The structure is more specific now.

[PAUSE 2s]

Begin with three minutes of extended exhale breathing.

[PAUSE 2s]

Then begin solo arousal work. Light grip, varied pace.

[PAUSE 2s]

This time, your specific job is to climb to a six. Stay there for thirty seconds. Then deliberately stop all physical stimulation. Take three slow exhales. Wait until your arousal level drops back down to a four — usually within thirty to ninety seconds. Then resume.

[PAUSE 3s]

Climb to six again. Hold. Stop. Drop. Resume.

[PAUSE 2s]

Do this cycle three to five times. Then either finish the session or stop without finishing — both are fine.

[PAUSE 3s]

A few specifics.

[PAUSE 2s]

The "stop" is complete. Hands off. The point is to feel arousal go down through the absence of stimulation, with the breath assisting.

[PAUSE 2s]

The "drop" usually happens faster than you'd think. Most men assume they need a long break to come back from a six. In practice, twenty to forty-five seconds is often enough. Let your body show you.

[PAUSE 2s]

If you accidentally cross seven and start heading toward eight, stop and let it drop fully — to three or below — before resuming. Don't try to power through the climb.

[PAUSE 3s]

Honest expectations.

[PAUSE 2s]

The first time, you'll probably misjudge levels. You'll think you're at a five and discover you've passed seven. You'll stop too late. You'll restart too fast. All of this is normal.

[PAUSE 2s]

The skill is: read level → stop → breathe → wait → reassess → resume. Each cycle is a rep. The reps build the awareness.

[PAUSE 3s]

A subtle point. The most important part of stop-start is *the wait*. Most men's instinct is to resume quickly — get back in the action. Resist that. The wait is where the nervous system practices coming down. Without that practice, you're just doing pause-and-rush.

[PAUSE 3s]

Do this practice once or twice this week. Don't do it daily — you want the body to have novelty between sessions.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow, the cognitive layer.

[PAUSE 2s]

Good work today.

# Session 13 — "Thoughts during sex"

**Day:** 13
**Duration:** ~7 min
**Type:** Lesson
**Goal:** Address the cognitive layer — what to do with the thoughts that show up during arousal. Builds on the spectatoring concept from Week 1 Day 4.

---

Today, the cognitive layer.

[PAUSE 2s]

Last week we talked about spectatoring — the part of you that pulls back during sex and watches from the outside, asking how things are going. Today we go deeper.

[PAUSE 3s]

Specifically: what do you do with the thoughts that show up during arousal? Especially the unhelpful ones.

[PAUSE 3s]

Most men with your profile have a familiar internal soundtrack during sex.

[PAUSE 2s]

Don't finish too fast. Slow down. Are they enjoying this? You've been at this for two minutes already. What's that feeling? Am I getting close? Don't get close. Think about something else. Think about taxes. No, that's not working. What time is it. They're going to be disappointed. Not yet, not yet, not yet.

[PAUSE 3s]

Sound familiar?

[PAUSE 2s]

Here's what's going on. The brain is trying to help. It's trying to manage the situation by thinking about it harder. The problem: thinking about sex during sex is the exact mechanism that triggers sympathetic activation, which triggers ejaculation faster.

[PAUSE 3s]

The instinct is to argue with these thoughts. To replace them. To force-think different thoughts. Mental algebra. Tax calculations. Fishing trips.

[PAUSE 2s]

The replacement-thought strategy is a well-known dead end. It works briefly, then fails. The reason: replacement still keeps you in your head. You've just changed the channel. The fundamental problem — being out of the body — remains.

[PAUSE 3s]

The actual move is different.

[PAUSE 2s]

You don't argue with the thoughts. You don't replace them. You let them be there. And you redirect attention to something physical — sensation, breath, contact — without making it a fight.

[PAUSE 3s]

This is the same skill we've been training in mindfulness practice if you've opted in for that. Notice the thought. Don't push it away. Don't engage with it. Just come back to whatever you can feel.

[PAUSE 2s]

The skin under your hand. The breath in your belly. The texture of the surface beneath you. Anything physical.

[PAUSE 3s]

Why this works. Sympathetic activation needs cognitive engagement to maintain its grip. Once you're back in the body — even briefly — sympathetic drops. Once sympathetic drops, the ejaculation reflex moves further away. And the inner soundtrack quiets, because nothing is feeding it.

[PAUSE 3s]

A specific framing that helps. The thought "don't finish too fast" is the same kind of thought as "don't think about a pink elephant." It's a self-canceling instruction. The harder you try to follow it, the more it dominates your attention.

[PAUSE 2s]

So instead of trying to follow it — *let it pass through.* And come back to what you can physically feel.

[PAUSE 3s]

Practice this in a low-stakes way during the day, not just during sex. Notice when your mind is busy. Then come back to a single physical sensation — your feet on the floor, the temperature of your hands, the chair against your back. Don't argue with the busy. Just shift attention.

[PAUSE 2s]

Build that micro-skill outside of sex first. Then it transfers in.

[PAUSE 3s]

Tomorrow we close out week two.

[PAUSE 2s]
