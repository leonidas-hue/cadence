## Cross-variant patterns

Before going pass-by-pass, two patterns visible across all three variants:

**Pattern A: factual-claim drift in service of tone.** Same as Variant A. When I'm trying to make a session feel reassuring or definitive, I tend to harden hedged claims. "Most men with your profile" → "Most men with your profile, by week six, notice that…" The hedging is correct; the specificity is invented. Variant D has the most of this because the underlying empirical territory is genuinely contested.

**Pattern B: the universal/branched architecture is more complex than the QA system surfaces well.** Variant D has 16 branched sessions across weeks 1, 4, 5, plus universal weeks. Variant A has the partnered/declined/solo branching at week 3. When QA notes things like "Day 24 contains X," I have to be specific about which branch — and the Variant D file structure is large enough that some content drift between branches is possible without obvious detection.

### Variant B

**Strengths.** The voice holds together remarkably well across 56 sessions. Specific things that work:

**Issues found.**

**🔵 #15 — "Most men with your profile" frequency**

Counted across Variant B: 12+ instances. This is a load-bearing phrase for the voice — establishes commonality, normalizes the user's experience. But at 12+ instances across 56 sessions, it could become rote. Worth alternating with: "Many in your profile…" / "It's common to…" / "Often what we see is…" Not urgent.

**🔵 #11 — Day 22 promises more arousal-focused content than weeks 5–8 deliver**

Day 22 (week 4 foundation) tells the user: "This is the most uniquely Variant B work in the protocol… we'll keep building on it." Weeks 5 and 6 each have one arousal-focused session (Days 30 and 39). Week 7 has one extended practice (Day 46). Week 8 has none.

Strictly speaking, this isn't a contradiction — the practice continues, just not as the headline of every week. But Day 22's framing implies it'll be the recurring centerpiece; the actual structure tapers.

**Fix:** soft adjustment to Day 22's framing — change "we'll keep building on it" to "we'll keep refining this skill across the rest of the program, in addition to broader integration work."

**🔵 #13 — Day 49 current path — "Option 3" framing**

Day 49's post-experiment framework is mostly excellent — three options, neutrally presented. But the description of Option 3 ("Resume previous frequency") is loaded:

The trade-off is that the conditioning patterns the protocol addressed often re-establish over four to eight weeks of returning to previous frequency. The behavioral skills you've built remain, but they're working against the same headwind they were addressing."*

This still names the trade-off but matches the structural neutrality of Options 1 and 2.

## PASS 2 — Factual accuracy / claims audit

### The big issue — Variant D's empirical foundation

**🔴 #1 — The "porn rewires the nervous system, partnered sex feels different" framing is presented as established fact when the research is genuinely contested**

This is the most serious issue across the three variants and warrants its own treatment.

Variant D's entire conceptual scaffolding rests on a specific empirical claim:

> *"Your nervous system is highly trainable. It calibrates to the stimuli you give it most often. If you give it a particular kind of input regularly — high-frequency, high-intensity, visually rich, with specific physical patterns — it adjusts its baseline. After a while, it considers that level of stimulus normal. The catch: real partnered sex usually doesn't match that baseline. Real sex is slower. Less visually intense. Different pressure. Different pace. So when you have partnered sex, your nervous system either fires too fast — because the contrast tips it over the edge — or sometimes the opposite, you struggle to maintain arousal because the input doesn't match what you've trained for."*

This is presented in Day 1 as a mechanical description of what's happening. The 8-week protocol then assumes this framing throughout.

**The actual published evidence on porn use and partnered sexual function is far more contested than this framing suggests.**

Specifically:
- The 2024 systematic review *Pornography and Sexual Dysfunction: Is There Any Relationship?* concludes "consensus about whether pornography use affects sexual functioning during partnered sex is lacking"
- Multiple recent systematic reviews find mixed associations — some studies show negative, some positive, some neutral
- The 2021 *Pornography Use and Holistic Sexual Functioning* systematic review (44 studies) finds that "in some situations, pornography use is associated with greater sexual functioning… in other cases it seems to be associated with lower sexual functioning"
- Studies in women specifically often find *positive* associations between porn use frequency and sexual function

The framing I use is one plausible mechanism among several, and it leans on a "habituation/sensitization" model that has popular currency but isn't well-validated for partnered sexual function specifically.

**This isn't catastrophic** — the *practices* in Variant D (grip recalibration, pace variation, visual stimulus reduction) work for users who have grip/pace/dependency conditioning patterns regardless of whether the cause framing is precisely right. But the *explanatory framing* in Day 1 and reinforced throughout is overconfident.

**Recommended approach:** soften the framing without abandoning it. Day 1's mechanical description should be presented as the working hypothesis, not the proven mechanism. Something like:

> *"Your nervous system is trainable. It calibrates to the stimuli you give it regularly. The leading hypothesis for porn-conditioned PE — and one that fits many men's experiences — goes like this: if you give your nervous system high-frequency, high-intensity visual input regularly, the baseline of what registers as 'arousing' shifts. Real partnered sex, which is usually slower and less visually intense, may then not match that shifted baseline. Some men experience this as faster ejaculation; some as difficulty maintaining arousal; some as both depending on context. The published research on this is genuinely mixed — some studies find these associations, others don't — but the practices we'll do in this protocol address conditioning patterns that show up in your screening regardless of the precise mechanism."*

This frames it honestly: a working hypothesis that fits, with practices that address the patterns regardless. Same content, more honest scaffolding.

**Estimated rewrite effort:** moderate. Day 1 (both versions) needs the largest update; Days 2–7 of week 1 need lighter touches; weeks 2–8 mostly stand because they're pattern-focused rather than mechanism-focused. Maybe 30–60 minutes of careful editing.

**🟡 #2 — Day 1 already implicitly contradicts itself**

Day 1 explicitly says:

> *"It's not a porn-is-evil protocol. I'm not going to tell you porn rewires your brain into a chemical wasteland. The actual research on that is weaker and more contested than the popular narrative suggests."*

This is good — it's honest about the literature. But then immediately afterward, the same session goes on to describe the rewiring framing as if it were established fact ("Your nervous system… calibrates to the stimuli…"). The user could reasonably notice this tension.

If we apply the fix in #1, this self-contradiction also resolves. The two issues are linked.

**🟡 #3 — Day 15 invented timeline claim**

> *"The improvement curve for your profile usually shows up between weeks four and six, when stimulus changes start having compounding effects."*

Same issue as in Variant A's QA — there's no clinical literature that establishes this specific timeline for porn-conditioned PE, because the diagnostic category itself isn't well-established in the literature. The timeline is a writer's intuition, not a research finding.

**Fix:** soften to "Most users start to notice changes by week four to six, though everyone's timeline is different" — same fix pattern as Variant A.

**🟡 #4 — Day 32 current path: invented neuroscience claim**

> *"The neuroscience isn't perfectly settled, but the clinical reasoning is straightforward. Sustained absence of high-stimulus input gives the nervous system a window to recalibrate. Less than two weeks tends not to produce measurable shift in most men with significant conditioning. More than two weeks adds little additional benefit and starts becoming about willpower endurance."*

I correctly hedge ("isn't perfectly settled") and then make a quantitative claim about the two-week duration as if it were derived from clinical evidence. It isn't. The two-week duration is a reasonable design choice for a behavioral protocol, not a research finding.

**Fix:** drop the false-precision phrasing. Something like:

> *"The two-week duration is a design choice for this protocol — short enough to be doable, long enough that the nervous system has a sustained period without high-stimulus input. We don't have clean clinical data on the optimal duration for this kind of taper; two weeks is what makes sense given everything else we're trying to hold together."*

Honest about the choice being a choice.

### Variant C — Day 30 minor calibration issue

**🟡 #5 — Day 30: dapoxetine trial numbers slightly off**

> *"The largest published clinical trial, involving over a thousand men across twenty-two countries, found mean IELT increased from about a minute at baseline to approximately three minutes on thirty milligrams, and three and a half minutes on sixty milligrams, after twelve weeks of as-needed use."*

Cross-checking against the actual McMahon 2009 paper (the 22-country trial, n=1162):
- Baseline: 0.9 min — checks out
- 30 mg at endpoint: 3.2 min (mean), 1.8 min (geometric mean) — my "approximately three" is fine for the mean
- 60 mg at endpoint: 3.5 min (mean), 2.3 min (geometric mean) — "three and a half" is correct
- Placebo at endpoint: 1.9 min (mean), 1.1 min (geometric mean) — I mention this elsewhere

The numbers are approximately right but I should specify "mean" not just give the number — geometric means are meaningfully lower and worth noting. Also the "twelve weeks" claim should be "twelve weeks" or "twenty-four weeks" (the trial has both endpoints; effects similar at both).

**Fix:** add clarity. "Mean IELT increased from about a minute at baseline to approximately three minutes on thirty milligrams, three and a half on sixty, after twelve weeks of as-needed use, with similar magnitude at twenty-four weeks. (These are arithmetic means; geometric means are somewhat lower, but the magnitude of effect over placebo is similar.)"

Optional. Probably overprecise for an audio script. Better: just leave the existing wording and accept it's approximate. The user doesn't need geometric vs arithmetic mean.

### Issues found

**🟡 #7 — Variant C: PFM volume drop-off between Day 36 and Day 51 not bridged**

Day 36 (week 6) tells the user:

> *"Pelvic floor work goes up one final time… After this week, pelvic floor work stays at maintenance dose for the rest of the program."*

This commits to a specific dose for weeks 6, 7, 8 — implicitly the same as week 5 plus the new mid-activity Kegels.

Day 51 (week 8 maintenance) gives the post-program dose:

> *"Two minutes of pelvic floor work. The reduced version of what you've been doing — five slow contractions, ten quick, three reverse Kegels."*

This is dramatically reduced from the established volume (which was two daily sessions of 10 slow + 20 quick + reverse Kegels, plus integrated reps in life).

**The drop-off isn't explained.** A user reading week 6 would expect to keep the elevated dose forever; the week 8 maintenance dose is presented as just-the-maintenance-dose, not as a new lower target.

**Fix:** add one or two sentences to either Day 51 or Day 50 explicitly bridging this. Something like: "Note that the maintenance dose is meaningfully lighter than what you've been doing during the protocol weeks. The protocol dose was building strength; once the strength is built, maintenance is much smaller. Don't keep doing the higher dose past week 8 — it's not necessary and tends toward over-training."

Already noted above in Pass 1. Same fix — soften Day 22's framing slightly.

## PASS 5 — Tonal failure-mode check

### Variant B

**🟡 #6 — Day 17 hypercontrol session uses partner-loaded examples**

Day 17 ("Hypercontrol") asks the user to identify control patterns and try a one-week experiment of releasing one. The examples given:

> *"Examples. The exact way the dishwasher gets loaded. The specific brand of toothpaste. Whether the laundry gets folded a particular way. The order in which weekend errands happen. The temperature in a room when someone else has set it."*

> *"Let your partner load the dishwasher their way. Use the toothpaste they bought. Don't refold the laundry."*

All examples assume cohabitation with a partner. For users who live alone, these examples don't apply. For users in early-relationship phases, they don't either.

**Fix:** add a single line acknowledging this and offering alternative examples. Something like:

> *"If you live alone, the examples translate — let a takeout order arrive in the wrong bag without complaining, use a different coffee than your usual, take a different route home from work, leave dishes overnight without organizing them. The experiment is the same; the targets are different."*

**🔵 #10 — Day 16 vigilance markers list could feel diagnostically judgmental**

Day 16 lists markers of generalized vigilance ("difficulty fully relaxing, easily startled, sleep that doesn't fully reset, trouble being alone with your thoughts, a sense that you have to keep an eye on things"). The framing is "if two or three of these resonate, vigilance is likely a baseline state for you."

This works as informative, but for users with significant anxiety or trauma history, reading a checklist of their experiences and being told it's a baseline state could feel pathologizing.

**Optional softening:** add a line at the end: "These markers don't define a disorder, and having several doesn't mean anything is wrong with you. They're patterns your nervous system has settled into, often for good reasons given what your life has involved. The protocol works with the patterns, not against you."

### Variant D

**⚪ #17 — Day 26 current path "what porn was doing for you" content needs clinician review**

Day 26 (current path) addresses the deeper question of what porn use was doing emotionally:

> *"For most heavy users, porn is doing several things simultaneously. Stress regulation… Boredom relief… Avoidance… Procrastination… Loneliness regulation."*

This is psychologically accurate for many users — these are real functions of compulsive behavior. But the framing comes close to clinical territory (compulsive behavior assessment, emotional regulation deficits).

**Recommendation:** before launch, have a sex therapist or licensed mental health professional review Day 26 specifically. The content isn't wrong, but a clinician's eye would confirm the framing is appropriate for an unsupervised app context vs warranting more explicit "talk to a therapist if this resonates" language.

I do include a mental health flag at the end of Day 26: *"If during this week you realize that porn was doing significant emotional regulation work… that's worth taking seriously. Therapy is a reasonable next step."*

So the structural protection is there. Just confirming with a clinician that it's calibrated correctly.

### Variant C

No tonal failure modes found that warrant action. The pharma module (which was the highest-risk content for tonal slips) maintains educational neutrality throughout.

## PASS 7 — Inclusivity and assumption check

**🟡 #8 — None of B, C, D include the diversity/inclusivity note we added to Variant A**

Variants B, C, D don't have this. The same gap exists.

**Fix:** apply the same insertion to each variant. The natural slot in each:
- **Variant B:** Day 15 (week 3) — "What maintains the clench" — has natural seam between the framing and the cognitive content
- **Variant C:** Day 36 (week 6) — "The last increase" — has space after the protocol overview
- **Variant D:** Day 36 (week 6 — current path version, paths converge) — same logic

The note text itself can be lightly customized per variant but the substance is the same as Variant A's:

> *"One thing to acknowledge before we go further. This program was written with the most common case in mind: a man with a female partner, having penetrative sex. That's the case most of the published research has studied. If you're in a different configuration — same-sex partnership, non-penetrative sex, polyamorous structure, or something else — the underlying work still applies. The breath, the awareness, the cognitive reframes — all apply regardless of the specifics. Where I use specific gendered language or assume penetrative-sex-as-the-frame, translate it to your situation. None of the core skills depend on you matching the example I'm describing."*
